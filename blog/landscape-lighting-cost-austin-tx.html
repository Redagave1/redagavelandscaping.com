Landscape Lighting Cost in Austin, TX: LED, COB & WLED

June 30, 2026

Landscape lighting is the single fastest way to transform how your property looks and feels. A house that disappears at sunset becomes a showpiece. Pathways go from dark and uncertain to inviting. Trees that you never noticed at night become sculptural focal points. And in Austin, where we spend more time outdoors than almost any other city in the country, lighting extends your usable yard by 5 to 6 hours every single day.

The question is: how much does it actually cost? The short answer: most professionally installed landscape lighting systems in Austin cost between $2,000 and $6,000 for a standard residential project. A front-yard-only package starts around $1,500. Full-property designs with premium fixtures and smart controls run $8,000 to $12,000 or more.

Here is a complete breakdown of what drives that number — with real pricing from Austin projects, an explanation of the different lighting technologies we install, and the hidden costs most quotes leave out.

Cost by Number of Fixtures

The simplest way to estimate your landscape lighting budget is by fixture count. Each professionally installed fixture — including the fixture itself, wiring, mounting, and aiming — costs $100 to $250 depending on fixture type and installation complexity.

Most Austin homes end up in the 15 to 30 fixture range. That covers architectural uplighting on the home, pathway lighting, tree accent lighting, and patio or outdoor kitchen illumination.

Not every fixture costs the same. Uplights and downlights require more precision and often need to be concealed, which drives up installation time. Path lights are faster to install. Here is what each type costs including installation:

Big-box fixtures use plastic housings that crack in Austin’s UV exposure within 2 to 3 years.

LED vs. COB vs. FCOB vs. WLED: What Is the Difference?

This is where the landscape lighting industry gets confusing. Most companies still install basic LED fixtures. We install WLED and FCOB — the next generation of lighting technology. Here is what each one actually means:

Standard LED — $50 to $200 per fixture

Individual LED diodes mounted on a circuit board. This is what 90% of landscape lighting companies install. It works fine for path lights and basic uplighting. You can see individual points of light if the fixture is visible, and the beam patterns are limited to what the fixture’s reflector produces. Lifespan is 25,000 to 40,000 hours. Bulbs cost $2 to $8 each.

COB (Chip on Board) — $80 to $300 per fixture

Multiple LED chips packed onto a single substrate. This produces a brighter, more uniform beam with better color rendering. COB fixtures are excellent for uplighting large trees, illuminating stone walls, and anywhere you need serious output from a small fixture. Higher lumens per watt than standard LED, and the beam quality is noticeably better.

FCOB (Flip Chip on Board) — $15 to $40 per linear foot

The latest generation. FCOB mounts LED chips facedown directly onto the substrate, eliminating wire bonds entirely. The result is a continuous, dot-free line of light that looks like neon but runs cool, uses almost no energy, and lasts 50,000+ hours. Unlike traditional LED strip lights that show individual dots, FCOB produces a smooth, unbroken glow.

This is what we integrate into steel planters, under cap stones, along retaining wall edges, and into pergola structures. The effect is dramatic — surfaces appear to float, edges glow, and the entire landscape takes on a sculpted, architectural quality that individual fixtures cannot produce.

WLED (White LED / WiFi LED) — $100 to $400+ per fixture

WLED can refer to white-specific LED systems optimized for color temperature accuracy, or to WiFi-addressable LED systems that can be controlled via app. The addressable systems let you adjust color temperature, brightness, create lighting scenes, and program schedules from your phone. A WiFi smart controller adds $300 to $600 to your system, and scene-based controllers with multiple zones run $800 or more.

A reputable company includes transformer, wiring, trenching, and labor in their per-fixture price. If you are getting a quote that only lists fixture cost, ask what the installed price is — the difference is usually 40% to 60% higher.

Color Temperature: What Kelvin to Choose

Color temperature is measured in Kelvin (K). It determines whether your landscape lighting looks warm and inviting or cold and commercial. Getting this wrong is one of the most common and most visible mistakes.

  • 2200K – 2500K (amber warm): Very warm, candlelight-like glow. Good for intimate patio areas and accent lighting. Can look too dim for pathway safety lighting
  • 2700K (warm white): The sweet spot for residential landscape lighting. Warm golden tone that enhances stone, wood, and plant material. Flattering to skin tones for outdoor entertaining. This is what we recommend for most Austin projects
  • 3000K (neutral warm): Slightly crisper than 2700K. Works well on modern or contemporary homes. Good for architectural uplighting where you want detail visibility without the coldness of higher temperatures
  • 4000K+ (cool white / daylight): Looks like a parking lot. Avoid this for residential landscape lighting entirely. It creates an institutional, unwelcoming feel and clashes with the warm tones of Austin’s native stone and wood

Our recommendation: 2700K for everything. Consistency across your entire property creates a cohesive look. Mixing color temperatures — 2700K in front and 3000K in back — creates a jarring visual disconnect that you notice every night.

What Landscape Lighting Actually Does for Your Home Value

Landscape lighting is not just aesthetics. It has a measurable impact on property value, security, and usability:

  • Home value increase: The National Association of Realtors reports that landscape lighting can increase home value by $2,500 or more. Homes with professional outdoor lighting see a 50% to 60% return on the lighting investment
  • Buyer perception: 41% of homebuyers rate outdoor lighting as essential or desirable according to the National Association of Home Builders. In competitive Austin neighborhoods like Tarrytown, Westlake, and Circle C, professional landscape lighting is an expectation, not a luxury
  • Security: A well-lit property is a harder target. Motion-activated floodlights and consistent perimeter lighting eliminate the dark zones that make homes vulnerable. Austin PD consistently recommends exterior lighting as a top crime deterrent
  • Usability: In Austin you can be outside comfortably from March through November. Landscape lighting extends your yard’s usable hours from sunset to bedtime — that is 4 to 6 extra hours every day during the months you actually want to be outside

Common Design Mistakes

We see these on nearly every property where a previous company installed lighting or where the homeowner did it themselves:

  • Too many fixtures, too close together. More lights does not mean better lighting. Overlapping pools of light flatten the landscape and create a runway effect. Good lighting design uses negative space — dark areas between lit areas — to create depth and drama
  • Fixtures aimed at the wrong angle. An uplight aimed straight up at a tree trunk looks like a searchlight. Aimed at 15 to 30 degrees off-center, it catches the canopy and creates natural shadow patterns. Aim matters more than wattage
  • Wrong color temperature. Mixing 3000K and 2700K fixtures, or using 4000K+ anywhere on a residential property, creates a cold, institutional look. Pick one temperature and stay consistent
  • Ignoring the transformer location. A humming transformer mounted on your back wall next to the patio ruins the ambiance you just paid to create. Tuck it behind landscaping, in a utility area, or inside a weatherproof enclosure
  • No plan for the trees. Trees are the single highest-impact element to light. One well-aimed uplight on a live oak creates more drama than ten path lights. If your lighting plan does not include tree lighting, rethink it
  • Lighting everything equally. Not everything deserves the same attention. Your front door, a specimen tree, and a stone wall are focal points. Your AC unit and garbage cans are not. Good lighting design is about hierarchy

Austin-Specific Considerations

Austin’s climate and landscape create specific conditions that affect your lighting system:

  • Live oaks: Austin is a live oak city. These trees have massive canopies that respond beautifully to uplighting. A single 20-watt LED uplight placed at the base of a mature live oak is worth more than a dozen path lights for visual impact
  • Limestone and native stone: Austin’s native limestone glows warm under 2700K light. If you have stone retaining walls, flagstone patios, or limestone veneer on your home, landscape lighting makes the investment in that stone visible 24 hours a day
  • Xeriscape-heavy yards: Xeriscape landscapes with gravel, steel edging, native grasses, and sculptural plantings look even better lit. FCOB strips under steel planter edges and uplights on agaves or yuccas create a modern desert aesthetic that is unique to this region
  • Heat: Austin’s summers push 105°F+. LED fixtures handle this without issue — they run cool and are unaffected by ambient heat. Older halogen systems, on the other hand, generated their own heat and shortened their lifespan in hot climates
  • Storms: Austin gets heavy downpours. All wiring connections must be waterproof, and fixtures need IP65 or higher ratings. Cheap fixtures with open wire nuts will corrode within one storm season
  • Fire ants: Fire ants nest in warm, dry spaces — including inside transformer enclosures and around in-ground fixtures. Sealed housings and periodic inspection prevent outages caused by ant damage to wiring

How Much Does It Cost to Run Landscape Lighting?

Almost nothing. A typical LED landscape lighting system with 15 to 20 fixtures costs about $5 per month in electricity. Even large systems with 40+ fixtures rarely exceed $20 per month. LED uses 75% to 80% less energy than the halogen systems that were standard 10 years ago.

Most systems run on a timer or photocell that activates at dusk and shuts off at a set time — typically midnight or 1 AM. That means your lights are only on for 4 to 7 hours per night depending on the season. At Austin’s electricity rates, that is pennies per night.
