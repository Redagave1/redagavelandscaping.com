Steel Landscape Edging Cost in Austin, TX

June 30, 2026

Steel edging is the detail that separates a designed landscape from a yard. It creates permanent, razor-sharp transitions between lawn and beds, hardscape and gravel, planting areas and pathways. In every signature project we build at Red Agave, steel edging is the element that holds the entire design together — clean lines that stay clean for decades.

The short answer on cost: steel landscape edging costs $5 to $11 per linear foot for materials and $10 to $28 per linear foot fully installed. A typical residential project with 180 linear feet of edging runs $1,800 to $5,040 installed. Corten (weathering steel) edging costs more at $8 to $15 per foot for materials, $15 to $40 installed — but it lasts 40 to 75 years.

Here is a full breakdown of steel edging costs by type, how it compares to every other edging material, and why it is the only option we recommend for Austin landscapes.

Not all steel edging is the same. The type of steel, the gauge (thickness), the height, and the finish all affect the price:

Our recommendation: corten steel for premium projects, galvanized 10-gauge for standard residential work. Corten’s warm rust-brown patina is a perfect fit for Austin’s Hill Country aesthetic, and its 40 to 75 year lifespan makes it a one-time investment. For standard projects, 10-gauge galvanized holds cleaner lines than 14-gauge and resists bending from mower strikes and foot traffic.

Plastic is the most popular edging material nationally because it is cheap and easy to install. But in Austin, plastic edging fails faster than almost anywhere because of three factors: UV degradation from intense sun exposure, heat expansion that causes buckling, and clay soil movement that pushes stakes out. We remove and replace failed plastic edging on nearly every project we take on.

Why Steel Edging Is the Standard for Austin Landscapes

Steel edging is not just a style preference — it is the most practical choice for Austin’s specific conditions:

  • Clay soil resistance: Steel driven with 12 to 18 inch stakes stays anchored through Austin’s wet-dry clay cycles. Plastic pops out. Concrete cracks. Steel flexes slightly without breaking, then holds its line
  • Heat tolerance: Steel does not expand, buckle, or warp in 100°F+ temperatures the way plastic does. The edging line you install in January looks the same in August
  • UV stability: Galvanized and powder-coated steel does not degrade under UV exposure. The zinc or powder coating protects the underlying metal for decades
  • Mower durability: Steel survives contact with string trimmers and mower wheels. Plastic cracks and chips on impact. This alone saves hundreds of dollars in replacement costs over the life of the landscape
  • Precision: Steel holds tighter, cleaner lines than any other material. The difference is visible — a steel-edged bed has a geometric precision that makes the entire landscape look intentional and maintained

Gauge, Height, and Finish: What to Specify

Gauge (Thickness)

The lower the gauge number, the thicker the steel:

  • 16-gauge: Thin, lightweight, easy to bend by hand. Fine for gentle curves and DIY projects, but bends easily on impact. Not recommended for Austin clay
  • 14-gauge: Standard residential grade. Good balance of flexibility and rigidity. Our minimum recommendation
  • 12-gauge: Heavy-duty. Holds very straight lines and resists bending from mower contact. Harder to curve without a bending tool
  • 10-gauge: Commercial grade. Extremely rigid, holds the straightest lines, lasts the longest. Our preference for all professional installations

Height

  • 4 inches: Standard for lawn-to-bed transitions where both surfaces are at the same grade. The most common height for residential work
  • 6 inches: Best for containing mulch, decomposed granite, or gravel that sits above the adjacent surface. Our most-used height
  • 8+ inches: For raised bed borders, retaining applications, or dramatic design statements. Significantly more expensive per foot

Finish

  • Galvanized: Zinc coating prevents rust. Silver appearance that blends into the landscape over time. Most affordable protective finish
  • Powder-coated black: Clean, modern look. The coating adds UV and scratch resistance. Hides the edging in dark mulch beds. Popular in contemporary designs
  • Corten (weathering steel): Develops a warm rust-brown patina over 6 to 12 months that stops further corrosion. The patina color complements Austin’s limestone, native plantings, and earth tones. Premium option
  • Raw steel: No protective coating. Rusts uniformly to create a natural patina, but unlike corten, the corrosion does not stop — raw mild steel will eventually rust through in 5 to 10 years. Apply a rust inhibitor if you want to slow this process

Steel edging is one of the more DIY-friendly landscaping materials, but the results vary significantly:

Material cost$5 – $11 / lf$5 – $11 / lf

Tools neededFlat shovel, rubber mallet, stakes ($20–$50)Included

DIY makes sense for short, straight runs of 16-gauge edging. For anything over 100 linear feet, curved sections, or heavier 10 to 12-gauge material, professional installation is worth the labor cost. The precision of professional installation is visible — wavy, inconsistent DIY edging undermines the entire landscape design.
