Luxury Landscape Consultation Checklist: What to Prepare Before Your First Meeting

July 8, 2026

A landscape consultation is a two-way interview. You are evaluating whether the firm understands your vision and can execute it. The firm is evaluating whether the project scope, budget, and timeline align with their capabilities. The more prepared you are walking in, the more productive that conversation will be — and the faster you will know whether you have found the right partner.

This is everything you should gather, consider, and ask before your first meeting with a landscape design-build firm in Austin.

Part 1: What to Gather Before the Meeting

Documents

  • Property survey / plat map — Shows lot boundaries, easements, setbacks, and utility locations. You received this when you purchased your home. If you cannot find it, your title company or Travis County records can provide a copy
  • HOA architectural guidelines — If your neighborhood has an HOA, bring the architectural review committee (ARC) guidelines. These dictate fence heights, material restrictions, color palettes, and approval timelines. Some HOAs require specific plant species or prohibit certain hardscape materials
  • Existing irrigation system info — Controller model, zone count, water source (city, well, reclaimed). If you do not know this, a good firm will assess it during the site visit
  • Recent utility bills — Water bills help estimate current irrigation costs and establish a baseline for water-saving designs like xeriscape

Inspiration

  • Photos of landscapes you love — Pinterest boards, Instagram saves, magazine tear-sheets, photos you have taken in neighborhoods. These communicate your aesthetic preferences far more effectively than verbal descriptions. “I want something natural” means different things to different people; a photo eliminates ambiguity
  • Photos of your current yard — From multiple angles, at different times of day (morning sun, afternoon shade patterns). Include problem areas: standing water, dying plants, ugly views, areas that get no use
  • Photos of things you do NOT want — Equally valuable. If you hate tropical-looking landscapes, round stepping stones, or bright-colored flowers, say so early

Site knowledge

  • Sun and shade patterns — Note which areas get full sun, partial shade, or deep shade at different times of day. This drives plant selection and outdoor living placement
  • Drainage issues — Where does water pool after rain? Where does roof runoff concentrate? Which areas stay soggy for days?
  • Existing trees to keep or remove — Austin has strict tree protection ordinances for trees over 19 inches in diameter. Know which trees are staying and which you want removed
  • Underground utilities — Gas, water, sewer, electric, cable, irrigation lines. Call 811 before any excavation, but knowing the general locations helps the design process
  • Soil conditions — If you have dug in your yard, note what you found: caliche, clay, rock, sandy soil. If you have not dug, the firm will assess during the site visit

Part 2: Define Your Priorities

Before the meeting, have a conversation with everyone in your household about how the outdoor space should function. The most common source of design friction is when the homeowner and their partner have different visions that have not been discussed.

Use and lifestyle questions

Priority ranking

If budget is a factor (and it always is), rank your priorities. What is non-negotiable, what is important, and what would be nice to have? A clear hierarchy allows a good designer to maximize impact within your budget.

Part 3: Budget — The Conversation Nobody Wants to Have

Budget is the most uncomfortable and most important topic in a landscape consultation. Many homeowners avoid stating a number because they fear the firm will design to the top of their range. Good firms do the opposite — they design to your priorities within your range, and they tell you when your wish list exceeds your budget so you can make informed decisions rather than discovering it at the proposal stage.

Austin luxury landscape cost ranges

Project ScopeTypical RangeWhat’s Included

Be honest about your range. A firm that does not ask about budget is either going to waste your time with an unaffordable design or deliver a generic proposal that does not reflect your priorities. A firm that asks about budget — and respects it — is the one you want designing your project.

Phasing: the smart way to manage large budgets

If your vision exceeds your current budget, a master plan with phased construction is the professional approach. The entire property is designed as a unified vision, then built in 2–3 phases over 1–3 years. Each phase is functional and complete on its own — you never live with a half-finished yard. The master plan ensures that Phase 2 infrastructure (gas lines, electrical conduit, drainage) is installed during Phase 1 so nothing has to be torn up later.

Part 4: Questions to Ask the Firm

Capability questions

  • “Can I see photos of projects similar to mine that you have completed?” — Not renderings. Built projects. A firm that cannot show completed work similar to your scope is not the right fit
  • “Do you design and build, or just one?” — Design-build firms control the entire process. Firms that only design hand the plans to a separate contractor, creating communication gaps. Firms that only build work from your plans (or have none)
  • “Who will be on my project daily?” — Meet the project manager and crew lead, not just the salesperson. The people who build it determine the quality
  • “What is your warranty?” — Industry standard is 1 year on hardscape workmanship, 1 year on plant material (with proper irrigation and maintenance). Some firms offer longer
  • “Are you licensed and insured?” — General liability ($1M minimum), workers' compensation for all crew, and any required city/state licenses. Ask for certificates

Process questions

  • “What does your design process look like?” — A good process includes: site analysis, concept development, design presentation with revisions, material selection, detailed scope of work, construction schedule. If the answer is “we can start next week,” that is a red flag
  • “Is there a design fee?” — Quality design takes time. Firms that design for free are either absorbing the cost (and marking up construction) or not actually designing. A design fee of $1,500–$5,000 for a comprehensive residential plan is standard and indicates the firm values the design process
  • “How do you handle change orders?” — Changes during construction are normal. The question is whether they are documented, priced, and approved before execution — or whether they appear as surprises on the final invoice
  • “What is your payment schedule?” — Standard: deposit (10–25%) to secure scheduling, progress payments tied to milestones, final payment at completion. Never pay 100% upfront. Never pay final payment before a walkthrough

Part 6: After the Consultation

What to expect next

  • Within 1 week: A follow-up from the firm summarizing what was discussed and confirming next steps
  • Within 2–4 weeks: A design concept or proposal, depending on the firm’s process
  • Design revisions: 1–3 rounds of revision are standard. Major vision changes may require additional design fees
  • Detailed scope of work: Before signing a construction contract, you should have a document specifying every material, every dimension, every system, and every cost. If it is not written down, it does not exist
