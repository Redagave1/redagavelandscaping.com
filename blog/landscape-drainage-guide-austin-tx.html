Austin Yard Drainage: French Drains, Dry Creek Beds & Grading

July 1, 2026

If you have standing water in your Austin yard after a rain, you have a drainage problem. If that standing water is near your foundation, you have an expensive problem. Austin sits on Blackland clay — a dense, expansive soil that absorbs almost no water when saturated and sheds runoff like concrete. That is why a 2-inch rainstorm in Austin creates the kind of flooding that a 4-inch storm would cause in sandy soil. The water has nowhere to go except across the surface, into low spots, and against your foundation.

This guide covers every residential drainage solution that works in Austin’s clay: French drains, surface drains, dry creek beds, grading, catch basins, and flow wells. We will explain what each one does, when to use it, what it costs, and how to avoid the installation mistakes that make drainage problems worse.

Why Austin Has Worse Drainage Than Most Cities

Three factors make drainage in Austin uniquely challenging:

Blackland clay soil

Austin’s dominant soil type is the Houston Black clay series — a vertisol that swells up to 30% when wet and shrinks when dry, creating deep cracks in summer that seal shut when the rains return. Saturated clay has a percolation rate near zero. Water does not soak in — it sits on top or moves laterally across the surface. This means standard drainage approaches designed for permeable soils (rain gardens alone, simple grading) are often insufficient in Austin. You need to physically move the water off the property with pipes and gravel.

Flash-flood rain events

Central Texas rain does not arrive gently. Austin averages 34 inches of annual rainfall, but much of it falls in intense bursts — 2 to 4 inches in under an hour during spring and fall storms. Drainage systems must be designed for peak flow, not average flow. A system that handles a half-inch rain but overflows during a 2-inch storm is poorly designed for this climate.

Foundation vulnerability

Austin’s expansive clay directly threatens your foundation. When one side of your slab stays wet while the other dries out, the clay under the wet side swells and the clay under the dry side contracts. This differential movement — sometimes measured in inches — cracks slabs, shifts piers, and causes structural damage that costs $5,000 to $30,000+ to repair. Drainage is not just a landscaping issue in Austin — it is foundation protection.

How to Diagnose Your Drainage Problem

Go outside during a heavy rain and observe:

  • Where water pools: Mark every area where standing water accumulates and does not drain within 30 minutes after rain stops
  • Where water flows: Watch the direction and speed of sheet flow across your yard. Water follows the path of least resistance — is it flowing toward your foundation or away from it?
  • Downspout discharge: Where does roof runoff go when it exits the downspouts? If it dumps against the foundation and pools, that is your primary problem
  • Neighbor’s runoff: Is water from an uphill neighbor’s property flowing into your yard? This is common in Austin neighborhoods with graded lots
  • Foundation perimeter: Is the soil within 10 feet of your foundation staying soggy? The recommended grade is a 1/4-inch drop per foot for the first 10 feet away from the foundation

Take photos and videos during the rain. A good drainage contractor will want to see exactly where water goes before proposing a solution.

Drainage Solution 1: Grading and Regrading

Grading is the most fundamental drainage solution — reshaping the soil surface so water flows where you want it to go. Before installing any pipe or drain, the grade should be corrected.

Foundation grading

The ground within 10 feet of your foundation should slope away at a minimum of 1/4 inch per foot (2% slope). For a 10-foot run, that is a 2.5-inch drop. Many Austin homes lose this grade over time as clay soil shifts and settles, allowing water to flow back toward the slab.

Yard grading

The overall yard should slope toward a drainage outlet — the street, a drainage easement, or a designated low point where a drain system can collect and discharge water. Flat spots anywhere in the yard become standing water during rain.

Drainage Solution 2: French Drains

A French drain is a gravel-filled trench containing a perforated pipe that collects subsurface water and routes it to a discharge point. It is the most common and versatile drainage solution for Austin yards.

How a French drain works

  1. Water saturates the soil around the trench
  2. Gravity pulls water through the gravel surrounding the pipe
  3. Water enters the pipe through perforations (holes face down so water seeps up into the pipe when the water table rises)
  4. The pipe slopes at a minimum 1% grade (1 inch per 8 feet) carrying water to the discharge point
  5. Water exits at a daylight outlet (street, lower area) or into a flow well

Why material choices matter in clay

  • Schedule 40 PVC vs. corrugated pipe: Corrugated pipe is cheaper ($0.50–$1/ft vs. $2–$4/ft) but the ridges trap fine clay sediment that clogs the pipe within 5–10 years. Smooth-wall PVC flushes clean and lasts 20–30 years
  • Washed gravel vs. crushed limestone: Crushed limestone produces fines (dust and small particles) that migrate into the pipe perforations and clog the system. Washed river rock stays clean
  • Geotextile fabric: In clay soil, this is not optional. Without fabric, clay particles migrate into the gravel within 2–3 years and reduce its drainage capacity to near zero. The fabric acts as a filter — water passes through, clay does not

Drainage Solution 3: Surface Drains and Catch Basins

Surface drains collect water from the surface — puddles, sheet flow, and patio runoff — through a grated inlet and pipe it to a discharge point using solid (non-perforated) pipe.

When to use surface drains

  • Low spots in the yard where water pools on the surface
  • At the base of slopes where sheet flow collects
  • Along the edge of patios and driveways where water runs off the hardscape
  • At downspout locations to capture roof runoff

Components

  • Catch basin: A box with a grated top that collects surface water. Common sizes are 9×9, 12×12, and 18×18 inches. Larger basins handle more flow
  • Channel drain: A long, narrow grated trench drain for linear applications (along patio edges, driveway transitions). Also called a trench drain
  • Solid pipe: 4-inch PVC (non-perforated) routes water from the catch basin to the discharge point
  • Pop-up emitter: A spring-loaded outlet at the end of the pipe run that opens when water flows and closes when it stops, preventing debris from entering the pipe

Drainage Solution 4: Dry Creek Beds

A dry creek bed is a rock-lined channel that routes surface water along a natural-looking path. When it rains, it functions as a drainage channel. When it is dry, it looks like a decorative landscape feature.

When dry creek beds work

  • Routing water from one part of the yard to another along a natural slope
  • Handling downspout runoff with a visible, attractive discharge path
  • Replacing eroded swales with a stabilized channel
  • Areas where underground pipe cannot be run (tree roots, rock, access issues)

How to build a dry creek bed that actually drains

  1. Grade the channel: Minimum 1–2% slope from inlet to outlet. Without proper slope, the creek bed becomes a pond
  2. Excavate: 12–18 inches deep, with a flat or slightly concave bottom
  3. Line with geotextile fabric: Prevents clay from migrating up through the rock and clogging the channel
  4. Layer the rock: Large boulders on the edges (6–12 inch), medium cobble in the channel (3–6 inch), river rock in the center (1–3 inch). This mimics natural creek structure and handles different flow rates
  5. Define inlet and outlet: Where does water enter the creek bed, and where does it exit? Both points need to be intentional, not just where the rock ends
  6. Plant the edges: Native grasses and perennials along the edges (inland sea oats, Mexican feather grass, frogfruit) stabilize the banks and make it look like a natural feature

Drainage Solution 5: Downspout Extensions and Redirects

Roof runoff is the single largest source of water near your foundation. A 2,000 square foot roof sheds approximately 1,250 gallons per inch of rainfall. If your downspouts dump that water against the foundation, no amount of yard drainage will compensate.

  • Minimum extension: Downspouts should discharge at least 10 feet from the foundation. Further is better
  • Underground routing: Solid 4-inch PVC pipe from the downspout base to a pop-up emitter near the street or in a drainage area. $20–$28 per linear foot installed
  • Surface extensions: Splash blocks or corrugated flex pipe above ground. Cheap ($5–$20) but ugly and often kicked or moved. Underground routing is the permanent solution

Drainage Solution 6: Flow Wells (Dry Wells)

When you cannot daylight a drain to the street or to a lower area of the property, a flow well provides an underground discharge point. It is a buried chamber (typically 30–50 gallon capacity) surrounded by gravel that stores water and allows it to slowly percolate into the surrounding soil.

  • Cost: $400–$600 installed per well
  • Capacity: A single flow well handles approximately 50 gallons. Multiple wells can be chained for larger volumes
  • Austin caveat: Flow wells work best in soil with some permeability. In heavy clay, percolation is slow and the well may not empty between storms. In these cases, connecting to the street drain is the better option

Most Austin properties need a combination of solutions — rarely does a single drain fix everything. A typical residential drainage project includes regrading around the foundation, extending 2–4 downspouts, and installing 50–100 feet of French drain or surface drain pipe.

Common Drainage Mistakes in Austin

  1. Using corrugated pipe in clay: Corrugated pipe traps sediment in the ridges. Use smooth-wall PVC
  2. Skipping geotextile fabric: Without it, clay migrates into the gravel and clogs the system within 2–3 years
  3. Insufficient slope: Pipe must have a minimum 1% grade. Zero slope means standing water inside the pipe, which defeats the purpose
  4. Crushed limestone instead of washed gravel: Limestone fines clog perforations. Washed river rock stays clean
  5. Draining onto the neighbor’s property: This is against City of Austin regulations and will create a dispute
  6. Ignoring the discharge point: Where does the water go? If the pipe ends in a garden bed, you have moved the puddle, not solved the problem
  7. Installing drainage after the landscape is finished: Drainage should be the first phase of any landscape project. Trenching through a finished patio or planted beds is expensive and destructive
  8. Foundation drains too shallow: Foundation perimeter drains need to be 24–36 inches deep to intercept water before it reaches the slab. A 12-inch drain sits above the problem
