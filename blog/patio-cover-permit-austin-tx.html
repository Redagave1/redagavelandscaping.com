Do You Need a Permit for a Patio Cover in Austin, TX?

July 3, 2026

The short answer is: if it is attached to your house, yes. If it is freestanding and small, maybe not. But the permit is often the easy part. The harder question in Austin is whether your property has enough impervious cover remaining to build a patio cover at all. Here is the complete guide.

Impervious Cover: Austin’s Biggest Constraint

In most Texas cities, a patio cover permit is straightforward paperwork. In Austin, the real constraint is impervious cover — the percentage of your lot covered by surfaces that prevent rainwater from soaking into the ground. Austin enforces impervious cover limits more aggressively than nearly any other city in Texas.

What counts as impervious cover

  • Your house footprint
  • Driveway and garage
  • Sidewalks and walkways
  • Patios (concrete, pavers, stone)
  • Pool decks and coping
  • Solid-roof patio covers and carports
  • Any surface that sheds water rather than absorbing it

What typically does not count

  • Open-beam pergolas with no solid roof
  • Gravel surfaces (depending on base preparation)
  • Permeable pavers (with proper drainage certification)
  • Decks elevated above grade with open spacing (may qualify for partial credit)
  • Landscape beds and turf

On a typical 7,000-square-foot lot in an SF-3 zone with a 45% impervious cover limit, you have a maximum of 3,150 square feet of impervious surface. If your house, driveway, and existing sidewalks already use 2,800 square feet, you have only 350 square feet remaining — barely enough for a modest patio, let alone a patio with a solid-roof cover.

How to check your remaining impervious cover

  1. Find your lot size on your property survey or Travis County Appraisal District records
  2. Determine your impervious cover limit from your zoning and watershed
  3. Calculate your current impervious cover (all existing hard surfaces)
  4. The difference is what you have available for new construction

If you are close to your limit, an open-beam pergola is the solution. It provides shade structure without adding impervious cover. Pair it with a permeable patio surface and you may be able to build where a solid-roof cover would put you over the limit.

Attached vs Freestanding: How the Code Sees Them

Attached patio covers

Any structure connected to your house with a ledger board is considered an extension of the house. This means:

  • It follows the house’s setback requirements (not the more generous accessory structure setbacks)
  • It requires a building permit with structural drawings
  • The structural drawings must show how the ledger board connects to the existing wall framing
  • If the structure exceeds 20 feet in height, drawings must be sealed by a Texas-registered engineer or architect
  • An inspection is required after construction

Freestanding structures

A structure with its own posts and foundations, not physically connected to the house, is classified as an accessory structure. The key exemption:

  • A one-story detached accessory structure that is 200 square feet or less, 15 feet tall or less, has no plumbing, and is not in a floodplain may be exempt from a building permit
  • Even exempt structures must meet setback requirements and count toward impervious cover (if they have a solid roof)
  • Freestanding structures over 200 square feet require a permit

A freestanding steel or aluminum pergola under 200 square feet with open beams (no solid roof) is the easiest patio cover to build in Austin — no permit, no impervious cover impact, minimal setback concerns.

The Permit Process

What you need to submit

  1. Site plan: Shows the patio cover location relative to property lines, the house, and other structures with dimensions
  2. Structural drawings: Foundation details, post sizes, beam sizes, connection details, and roofing specifications
  3. Impervious cover calculation: Current and proposed impervious cover percentages
  4. Tree survey: If any protected trees (19 inches or larger in diameter) are within the construction area, a tree permit may be required
  5. Engineering seal: Required if the structure exceeds 20 feet in height or if the project is complex

Timeline

Plan to submit permits 3–4 weeks before your target construction start date for a straightforward patio cover. If tree permits or historic district reviews are involved, allow 6–8 weeks.

Permit costs

Austin building permit fees for a residential patio cover typically run $200–$600, depending on the project value. Plan review fees and inspection fees are included. Tree permits, if required, add $50–$200.

HOA Considerations

If your property is in an HOA, you need Architectural Control Committee approval in addition to any city permits. HOAs typically regulate:

  • Material: Many HOAs require the patio cover to match the house’s exterior materials or color scheme
  • Roofing: Some HOAs require the patio cover roof to match the main house’s shingles
  • Height: May limit the structure to the house’s roofline or lower
  • Placement: May restrict construction to the rear yard only
  • Visibility: The structure may not be visible from the street in some communities

Submit your HOA application and city permit application at the same time to avoid doubling the wait.

Patio Cover Permit Checklist

  1. Calculate your current and proposed impervious cover
  2. Confirm your property’s zoning and setback requirements
  3. Check floodplain status
  4. Identify any protected trees in the construction area
  5. Check for historic district requirements
  6. Determine if the structure is attached or freestanding
  7. Prepare structural drawings (engineer-sealed if over 20 ft)
  8. Submit HOA application (if applicable)
  9. Submit city permit application
  10. Schedule inspections after construction
