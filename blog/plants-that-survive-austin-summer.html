25 Plants That Survive an Austin, TX Summer

July 3, 2026

Austin averages 45 days above 100°F per year, and water restrictions limit automatic irrigation to one day per week. A landscape that depends on three watering days to look good is not designed for Austin. These 25 plants do not just survive summer — they look better in it. Every plant on this list is proven in Central Texas heat, alkaline clay soil, and extended drought.

Why these work

Native and adapted perennials survive Austin summers because they evolved in this climate. They go semi-dormant during the worst heat, reducing water demand, then rebound when conditions improve. Most bloom heaviest in the 90–100°F range when other plants shut down. Plant them in fall and give them one mild season to establish roots before their first summer.

The star: Texas sage

Texas sage is the single most drought-tolerant ornamental shrub for Austin. It demands full sun and poor, rocky soil — overwatering and rich soil actually harm it. After a summer rain event, the entire plant explodes with purple flowers. The worse the drought, the more spectacular the show when rain finally arrives. It handles 100°F heat without flinching and looks better in August than in April.

Plant trees in October through November. Fall-planted trees develop 3–4 months of root growth before summer and survive their first hot season at dramatically higher rates than spring-planted trees. Water deeply once per week through the first year, regardless of season.

Ornamental grasses are the backbone of xeriscape design. They need no supplemental irrigation once established, provide year-round structure, and require almost zero maintenance — cut them back to 6 inches in late February and they regrow. Gulf muhly’s fall bloom is one of the most striking displays in Austin landscaping.

How to Establish Heat-Tolerant Plants

Even the toughest drought-tolerant plant needs water to establish. The goal is to get roots deep into the soil during a mild season so the plant is self-sufficient by summer.

  1. Plant in fall: October–November is ideal. The soil is warm, air is cool, and fall rains help with establishment
  2. Water weekly for the first season: Deep, infrequent watering forces roots downward. Shallow daily watering creates surface roots that fail in summer
  3. Mulch 3–4 inches: Mulch reduces soil temperature by 10–20°F and cuts evaporation by 50–70%
  4. Do not amend clay soil in planting holes: Amended holes create a “bathtub effect” where roots circle in the soft soil and refuse to grow into surrounding clay. Backfill with native soil
  5. Right plant, right place: Full-sun plants in full sun. Shade plants in shade. A shade-loving plant in full Austin sun will struggle no matter how tough it is
  6. Do not overwater drought-tolerant plants: Once established, these plants are more likely to die from overwatering than underwatering. Root rot from soggy soil kills more xeriscape plants than drought does

Designing with Heat-Tolerant Plants

A drought-tolerant landscape is not a compromise — it is a design decision. The best Austin landscapes combine xeriscape beds with gravel zones, hardscape, and landscape lighting to create outdoor spaces that look the same in August as they do in April. No brown patches.
