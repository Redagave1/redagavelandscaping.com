COB vs LED Landscape Lighting: FCOB, WLED & What Works

July 1, 2026

If you have researched landscape lighting recently, you have seen terms like COB, FCOB, WLED, SMD, and color temperature thrown around with minimal explanation. The lighting industry has undergone a technology shift in the last few years, and the new options — particularly FCOB strip lighting — produce dramatically better results than the traditional LED strips and fixtures that dominated five years ago.

This guide explains what each technology actually is, where to use it, what it costs, and how to combine them for a landscape lighting design that transforms your Austin outdoor living space after dark.

The Technology: SMD vs. COB vs. FCOB

All of these are LED technologies. The difference is how the LED chips are arranged on the strip or in the fixture.

SMD (Surface-Mounted Diode) — Traditional LED strips

  • Individual LED chips mounted at regular intervals along a flexible strip
  • Each chip is a distinct point of light, creating visible “dots” when the strip is exposed
  • Dot spacing varies — 30, 60, or 120 LEDs per meter. Higher density reduces but does not eliminate the dotting effect
  • Requires a diffuser channel to smooth the light, which adds bulk and cost
  • The most affordable LED strip option

COB (Chip on Board)

  • Hundreds of tiny LED chips packed directly onto the strip substrate, much closer together than SMD
  • Produces a more uniform light with significantly less visible dotting
  • Still has minor hot spots at very close viewing distances, but far smoother than SMD
  • Better heat dissipation than SMD due to the board-mounted design
  • Mid-range pricing between SMD and FCOB

FCOB (Flip Chip on Board) — Current best

  • LED chips are flipped upside down and mounted directly to the substrate without wire bonds
  • Produces a completely dot-free, continuous ribbon of light — no hot spots, no dark gaps, no visible individual LEDs
  • Superior heat dissipation because the chip’s heat-generating junction faces the substrate directly
  • Available in IP65 (weatherproof) for outdoor landscape use
  • Rated for 50,000+ hours (15–20 years at 8 hours per night)
  • The premium option, but the light quality difference is immediately visible

WLED: What It Means

WLED stands for Warm LED — LED fixtures with a color temperature in the warm white range (2700K–3000K). It is not a separate technology from the strips above — it is a designation for the warmth of the light output. Any SMD, COB, or FCOB strip can be WLED if it is manufactured at a warm color temperature.

In landscape lighting, WLED at 2700K is the standard. It produces the warm, amber glow that makes landscapes look inviting at night — the same tone as a candle or incandescent bulb. Avoid anything above 3000K for residential landscape lighting unless you want a cold, commercial feel.

Critical rule: Match all fixtures and strips to the same Kelvin temperature. Mixing 2700K uplights with 3000K under-cap strips creates a visible mismatch that looks unintentional. Pick one temperature for the entire system.

Landscape Lighting Fixture Types

FCOB strips handle linear applications beautifully, but a complete landscape lighting design also needs traditional fixtures for accent, task, and directional lighting:

Spotlights (uplights)

  • What they do: Sit at ground level and aim upward to illuminate trees, architectural features, columns, and textured walls
  • Effect: Dramatic shadows and highlights that define structure and depth. The most impactful single fixture type in landscape lighting
  • Placement: 1–2 feet from the base of what you are lighting, angled 30–60 degrees upward. For large trees, use 2–3 fixtures to light different sides
  • Cost: $50–$200 per fixture installed

Well lights (in-ground uplights)

  • What they do: Same function as spotlights, but recessed flush into the ground so only the lens is visible
  • Effect: Clean, modern look. Invisible during the day. The fixture disappears and only the light effect remains
  • Placement: Set into concrete, gravel, or planting beds. Requires a drainage-friendly installation so water does not pool in the housing
  • Cost: $100–$300 per fixture installed (more than spotlights due to excavation)

Path lights

  • What they do: Low fixtures along walkways, driveways, and garden edges that cast a downward pool of light
  • Effect: Functional wayfinding plus ambient ground-level glow. Avoid the “runway effect” of evenly spaced path lights in a straight line — stagger placement and vary spacing for a natural rhythm
  • Placement: 6–8 feet apart, alternating sides of a path. 12–18 inches from the path edge
  • Cost: $75–$200 per fixture installed

Downlights (moonlighting)

  • What they do: Mounted high in trees or under eaves, shining downward to simulate moonlight filtering through branches
  • Effect: Soft, dappled shadows on the ground. The most natural-looking landscape lighting technique. Creates a sense of depth and canopy
  • Placement: 15–25 feet high in mature tree canopies, angled slightly outward. Use 3000K for a cooler moonlight simulation
  • Cost: $150–$350 per fixture installed (includes tree mounting hardware)

Designing a Complete Lighting System

The best landscape lighting designs use a combination of fixture types and FCOB strips to create layers of light:

Layer 1: Architectural and tree lighting (spotlights/well lights)

Uplight the house facade, accent trees, and highlight focal points. This layer creates the dramatic nighttime identity of your property from the street and from inside the house looking out. Budget 3–8 fixtures for a typical Austin backyard.

Layer 2: Linear wash and glow (FCOB strips)

Under-cap lighting on retaining walls and seat walls, step lighting, and cove lighting under shade structures. This layer provides ambient background illumination and defines the edges of hardscape features. FCOB’s dot-free continuous output is transformative here — the wall appears to glow from within rather than being lit by individual points.

Layer 3: Pathway and functional (path lights and task lights)

Path lights along walkways for wayfinding and safety. Task lights at the outdoor kitchen for cooking. Step lights for grade changes. This layer is functional first, but contributes to the overall ambient light level.

Layer 4: Moonlighting (downlights in trees)

The finishing touch for properties with mature trees. Soft downward light through branches creates dappled shadows and a sense of natural nighttime canopy. Subtle, but it ties the entire lighting design together.

Technical Essentials

Transformer sizing

Low-voltage landscape lighting runs on 12V or 24V DC, stepped down from your home’s 120V AC by a transformer. Size the transformer at 80% of rated capacity — a 300-watt transformer should serve no more than 240 watts of fixtures. This provides headroom for future additions and prevents overloading. For larger systems, split across multiple transformers on separate circuits rather than one oversized unit.

Wire sizing and voltage drop

Over long wire runs, voltage drops and fixtures at the end of the run dim noticeably. Use 12-gauge wire for runs up to 100 feet and 10-gauge for runs up to 150 feet. For runs beyond 150 feet, use a hub-and-spoke wiring layout where the transformer feeds a central junction, and individual runs branch out to keep each run under 100 feet.

Connections

Every connection in a landscape lighting system is a potential failure point. Use waterproof, gel-filled wire connectors rated for direct burial — not standard wire nuts wrapped in electrical tape. Most landscape lighting failures trace back to corroded connections, not failed fixtures.
