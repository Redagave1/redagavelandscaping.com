Yard Drainage Solutions Cost in Austin, TX

June 30, 2026

Standing water in your yard is not just ugly — it kills grass, breeds mosquitoes, erodes landscape beds, and threatens your foundation. Austin’s Blackland clay soil makes drainage problems worse than almost anywhere else in the country. The clay absorbs almost no water during heavy rain, so nearly all of it runs off rather than soaks in. And limestone bedrock beneath shallow topsoil means there is often nowhere for water to go even if the clay did absorb it.

The short answer: yard drainage in Austin costs $2,000 to $10,000 for most residential projects. A simple French drain runs $1,500 to $5,000. A comprehensive drainage system with French drains, catch basins, and regrading costs $5,000 to $15,000. The average Austin homeowner spends around $4,500.

Here is a full breakdown of every drainage solution, what each costs, which ones work best in Austin’s clay soil, and how to avoid paying twice for a system that should have been designed right the first time.

Solution Deep Dives

French Drains — $15 to $50 per linear foot

The most versatile and widely used drainage solution. A French drain is a gravel-filled trench containing a perforated pipe that intercepts subsurface water and redirects it to an outlet. In Austin’s clay soil, a properly built French drain needs a trench 18 to 24 inches wide (versus 12 inches in sandy soil) filled with clean, washed gravel and wrapped in filter fabric to prevent clay migration into the gravel. The pipe must maintain a minimum 1% slope — 1 inch of drop per 8 feet of run — and discharge to daylight, a dry well, or a storm drain connection.

A typical 100-foot exterior French drain costs $1,500 to $5,000 installed. Labor accounts for 80% to 85% of the cost because digging in Austin clay is slow, heavy work that requires equipment. The gravel alone costs $10 to $60 per cubic yard, and a 100-foot drain requires 5 to 10 cubic yards depending on trench width.

Channel Drains — $30 to $150 per linear foot

Channel drains (trench drains) are linear grated drains set flush with the surface. They intercept sheet flow across driveways, patios, pool decks, and walkways. The grate material is the biggest cost variable: plastic grates run $30 to $50 per linear foot, metal grates $50 to $100, and heavy-duty cast iron grates for driveways $75 to $150. A typical driveway channel drain installation costs $400 to $2,500. Retrofitting a channel drain into existing concrete or pavers costs more than installing during new construction because the hardscape must be cut and patched.

Dry Creek Beds — $15 to $40 per linear foot

A dry creek bed is both a drainage feature and a landscape element. It handles moderate surface water flow through a shallow, rock-lined channel that looks like a natural creek bed year-round. Dry creek beds use a mix of river rock sizes (2 to 6 inches), large accent boulders, and native plantings along the banks. They work best for directing surface runoff through xeriscape and native plant beds. A 50-foot dry creek bed costs $750 to $2,000 for a basic installation, or $1,250 to $2,500 with boulders and plantings. They are not a substitute for a French drain when subsurface water is the problem — dry creek beds only handle surface flow.

Catch Basins and Surface Drains — $300 to $800 each

Catch basins are grated boxes installed at low spots where water collects. They connect to underground pipes that carry the water to an outlet. A single catch basin with 20 feet of connecting pipe costs $300 to $800 installed. Most comprehensive drainage systems use 2 to 4 catch basins at strategic low points, connected to a French drain or a dedicated outlet pipe. Catch basins are the fastest way to eliminate standing water at specific spots — the grate captures surface water before it has a chance to pond.

Regrading — $1 to $4 per square foot

Regrading is reshaping the ground so water flows away from structures rather than toward them. The land around a house must slope at least 6 inches within the first 10 feet from the foundation. Regrading one side of a foundation costs $500 to $1,000. Regrading around the entire house costs $1,000 to $3,000. A full backyard regrade costs $2,000 to $6,000 depending on yard size and severity. Regrading is often the most cost-effective first step because it uses gravity — no pipes, no structures, no maintenance. Many drainage problems can be solved or significantly reduced by regrading alone.

Dry Wells — $1,350 to $5,000

A dry well is an underground chamber that collects water and allows it to slowly percolate into the surrounding soil. Plastic-tank dry wells cost $1,350 to $2,500. Concrete dry wells cost $2,500 to $5,000. In Austin’s clay soil, dry wells are less effective than in sandy soil because clay does not absorb water quickly. They work best as overflow capacity within a larger system — paired with French drains that provide the primary drainage path. A dry well in clay soil needs to be significantly oversized compared to the same application in permeable soil.

Why Austin Drainage Costs More

Austin drainage projects cost 15% to 30% more than the national average. Here is why:

  • Clay soil: The Blackland clay prairie soil that covers much of Austin absorbs almost no water and is extremely difficult to dig. Trenching in clay takes 2 to 3 times longer than in sandy soil and requires heavier equipment
  • Wider trenches: French drains in clay need 18 to 24 inches of gravel versus 12 inches in permeable soil, doubling the gravel volume and trench width
  • Limestone bedrock: Shallow limestone bedrock in parts of West Austin, Westlake Hills, and the Hill Country requires rock cutting or routing around obstacles, adding $500 to $3,000
  • Flash flood intensity: Central Texas receives concentrated, high-intensity rainfall. A 2-inch storm in 30 minutes creates more runoff than the same 2 inches spread over 6 hours. Systems must be sized for peak flow, not average flow
  • Positive outlet requirement: Every drainage system needs somewhere for the water to go. In clay soil, that means a daylight outlet, storm drain connection, or oversized dry well — not just “let it soak in”

Common Austin Drainage Problems and Solutions

Standing water after every rain

Usually caused by a low spot in the yard combined with clay soil that will not absorb the water. Fix: catch basin at the low point connected to a French drain that carries water to a daylight outlet. Cost: $1,500 to $4,000.

Water pooling against the foundation

Caused by negative grading — the ground slopes toward the house instead of away. Fix: regrade to create positive slope (6 inches in 10 feet minimum) and extend downspouts underground to pop-up emitters at least 10 feet from the foundation. Cost: $1,000 to $3,500.

Soggy yard that never dries out

Caused by a high water table or subsurface water flow through clay layers. Fix: French drain system to intercept subsurface water before it reaches the problem area. May require a sump pump if there is no gravity outlet. Cost: $3,000 to $8,000.

Erosion on slopes

Caused by concentrated runoff from roofs, driveways, or upstream properties. Fix: retaining wall terracing to break the slope, dry creek bed to channel runoff, and plantings to stabilize soil. Cost: $3,000 to $15,000 depending on slope length and severity.

Driveway flooding

Caused by sheet flow across a flat or negatively graded driveway. Fix: channel drain across the driveway at the low point, connected to a French drain or catch basin. Cost: $800 to $3,000.

What a Complete Drainage System Includes

A well-designed Austin drainage system is not a single product — it is a combination of solutions working together:

  1. Regrading to establish positive slope away from all structures
  2. Downspout extensions carrying roof water 10+ feet from the foundation
  3. French drains intercepting subsurface water at problem areas
  4. Catch basins at low spots where surface water collects
  5. A positive outlet — daylight to a drainage easement, connection to storm drain, or dry well for overflow

Single-solution approaches often fail in Austin clay because the soil creates both surface runoff problems and subsurface water pressure simultaneously. A layered system addresses both.

DIY vs. Professional Installation

  • DIY-appropriate: Downspout extensions ($50 to $100 each in materials), small swales, and simple regrading with a shovel and rake. These do not require specialized equipment or engineering
  • Experienced DIY possible: A short French drain (under 50 feet) in non-clay soil. DIY materials cost $5 to $10 per linear foot. However, Austin’s clay is extremely difficult to dig by hand
  • Professional recommended: Any French drain in clay soil, catch basin systems, channel drains, and any project that connects to or affects neighboring properties. Incorrect slope, inadequate gravel, or a poor outlet will cause the system to fail within a year

Permits

Most residential yard drainage projects in Austin do not require a building permit. However, you need a permit if your property is in a floodplain, if the work involves connecting to the city storm drain system, if significant grading changes affect neighboring properties, or if the project is part of a larger development. Contact Austin Watershed Protection to confirm requirements for your specific property.
