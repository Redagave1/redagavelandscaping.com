Smart Drainage Engineering: French Drains, Dry Creek Beds & Roof Runoff Solutions for Austin

July 7, 2026

Austin averages 34 inches of rainfall per year — but that average hides the reality. In Central Texas, rain does not arrive gently over months. It arrives in violent, concentrated bursts: 2–4 inches in a single hour during spring and fall thunderstorms, sometimes 6+ inches in a day during tropical moisture events. Then weeks of nothing.

This feast-or-famine rainfall pattern, combined with clay and caliche soils that absorb almost nothing, means that drainage is not a maintenance issue in Austin — it is a structural engineering problem. Every drop that hits your roof, driveway, patio, and yard must go somewhere intentionally, or it will go somewhere destructively.

The national “standard” drainage solutions — 4-inch corrugated pipe, pop-up emitters, basic french drains — are designed for soils that absorb water and storms that deliver it gradually. In Austin, those systems are undersized by a factor of 2–4x on day one.

The Drainage Toolkit

1. Surface grading

Before any drain pipe goes in the ground, the finish grade of every surface must slope water away from structures. This is the foundation of all drainage engineering — everything else is supplemental.

  • Foundation perimeter: Minimum 6-inch fall in the first 10 feet away from the house (6% slope). This is the single most important grading requirement for foundation protection
  • Lawn areas: Minimum 2% slope (1/4 inch per foot) toward drainage collection points. Steeper is better on clay
  • Patios and hardscapes: Minimum 1% slope (1/8 inch per foot) away from house. Paver patios can tolerate steeper grades than mortared stone
  • Planting beds: Crown slightly above surrounding grade to prevent water from pooling against mulch and plant crowns

2. French drains

The workhorse of subsurface drainage. A French drain collects groundwater that has entered the soil and channels it to a discharge point.

Build
French Drain Cross-Section

Trench: 12–18 inches wide, 18–24 inches deep, sloped minimum 1% (1/8″ per foot) toward discharge. Filter fabric: Non-woven geotextile lining the entire trench (sides and bottom), with flaps left open at top. Gravel bed: 2–3 inches of clean ¾–1½ inch washed gravel on the bottom. Pipe: 4-inch rigid perforated PVC (Schedule 40 or SDR 35) laid holes-down on gravel bed. Not corrugated flex pipe — corrugated pipe crushes under soil load, collects sediment in corrugations, and cannot be cleaned. Backfill: Clean washed gravel to within 4–6 inches of grade. Fold fabric flaps over gravel. Top with 4–6 inches of topsoil or mulch.

Critical details:

  • Rigid PVC, not corrugated: Corrugated drain pipe is the number-one cause of failed French drains in Austin. It crushes, it clogs, it cannot be cleaned, and it fails in 3–7 years. Rigid PVC is permanent
  • Washed gravel, not road base: Road base (crushed limestone with fines) compacts into an impermeable mass. Washed gravel maintains void space for water flow permanently
  • Holes down: The perforated pipe sits on a gravel bed and collects water that rises to the pipe level. Holes-up catches surface water but is less effective at lowering the water table
  • Cleanout access: Install a vertical cleanout riser every 50 feet and at every change in direction for jetting maintenance

3. Channel drains

Channel drains (trench drains) intercept surface water flow before it reaches structures or problem areas. They are installed flush with the hardscape surface with a grate on top.

4. Dry creek beds

A dry creek bed is both a drainage channel and a landscape feature — when engineered correctly. The key word is engineered. A decorative creek bed with scattered river pebbles washes out in the first real storm. A structural creek bed handles serious water volume.

Build
Structural Dry Creek Bed

Excavation: 8–12 inches deep, 24–36 inches wide (sized to watershed). Sloped minimum 2% toward discharge. Base: 4 inches of compacted road base for stability. Filter fabric: Non-woven geotextile over compacted base (prevents soil migration into stone). Optional pipe: 4-inch rigid PVC beneath the stone for high-volume applications — the creek bed provides overflow capacity above. Stone layers: 4–6 inch boulders anchor the banks and prevent edge erosion. 3–5 inch cobble fills the channel center. 1–2 inch river rock fills gaps. Edges: Steel edging or large boulders define channel edges and prevent stone migration.

Design as landscape feature: The best dry creek beds are indistinguishable from natural Hill Country creek channels. Vary the width along the run (narrower where it enters, wider at the collection point). Set 2–3 large specimen boulders at curves. Plant moisture-tolerant native grasses (inland sea oats, Gulf muhly) and native perennials along the banks. The creek bed should look intentional and beautiful when dry — because it will be dry 350 days a year.

5. Downspout management

A 2,000-square-foot roof in a 2-inch rainfall event generates 2,500 gallons of water in a single hour. That volume concentrated at 4–6 downspout discharge points is the number one cause of foundation saturation in Austin.

The minimum standard: Every downspout on the house should discharge a minimum of 10 feet from the foundation via solid (not corrugated) underground pipe. This single improvement prevents more foundation damage than any other drainage measure.

The Foundation Connection

In Austin, drainage is foundation protection. Slab-on-grade homes on expansive clay are extraordinarily sensitive to moisture variation around the perimeter. When one side of a foundation stays wet while the other dries out, differential movement causes cracking, door jamming, and structural damage. Foundation repair averages $5,000–$15,000 in the Austin market — and the root cause is almost always inadequate drainage.

A $4,000–$8,000 drainage system that keeps moisture levels consistent around the foundation perimeter is the single best return on investment in any Austin landscape project. It protects the foundation, prevents plant loss from waterlogging, and eliminates standing water that breeds mosquitoes.
