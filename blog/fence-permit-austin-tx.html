Do You Need a Permit for a Fence in Austin, TX?

July 3, 2026

Short answer: probably not. Most residential fences in Austin do not require a city permit. But “no permit needed” does not mean “no rules.” The City of Austin regulates fence height, placement, safety features, and corner lot visibility. Your HOA likely regulates materials, color, and style. Here is everything you need to know before building.

When You Do Not Need a Permit

You can build a residential fence in Austin without a permit if all three of these conditions are true:

  1. The fence is under 7 feet tall at every point
  2. The property is not in a FEMA-designated floodplain
  3. The fence is not a solid fence over 6 feet along the public right-of-way (other than an alley)

Most standard 6-foot privacy fences on interior lots meet all three conditions. No permit application, no inspection, no fee.

To check whether your property is in a floodplain, use the City of Austin’s online floodplain map at austintexas.gov or call Austin Development Services at 512-978-4000.

Height Rules

Austin’s Land Development Code sets different height limits depending on where the fence is located on the property:

LocationSolid Fence MaxOrnamental Fence Max
Front yard42 inches (3.5 ft)42 inches (3.5 ft)
Side yard (property line)6 feetHigher with approval
Rear yard (property line)6 feetHigher with approval
Side/rear with grade change (≥1 ft)7 feetHigher with approval
On or within building setback8 feetHigher with approval

Ornamental fences are defined as fences with an open design that have no more than 20% solid material. Wrought iron, steel panel fences with open pickets, and similar designs qualify as ornamental and have more flexibility on height.

How height is measured

Fence height is measured from the natural grade on the higher side of the fence to the top of the fence. If your neighbor’s yard is 2 feet lower than yours and you build a 6-foot fence, it appears as an 8-foot fence from their side. The code measures from your side (the higher grade). Grade changes of 1 foot or more along the fence line allow sections to reach 7 feet to maintain a level top line.

Corner Lot Rules

Corner lots have additional restrictions. Austin requires a visibility triangle at street intersections to ensure drivers can see oncoming traffic and pedestrians.

  • The visibility triangle is typically measured 25 feet from the intersection along each street
  • Within this triangle, fences and landscaping cannot exceed 30 inches in height
  • This applies to fences, walls, hedges, berms, and any other visual obstruction
  • Contact Austin Transportation and Public Works before building a fence on a corner lot to confirm the exact triangle dimensions for your intersection

If your corner lot fence needs to transition from 30 inches (in the visibility triangle) to 6 feet (along the side property line), plan the transition point before construction. A stepped design or angled transition looks better than an abrupt height change.

Safe Fencing Code

The City of Austin updated its safe fencing requirements effective July 31, 2023. These rules apply to all new fence construction and to existing fences undergoing substantial repair or replacement.

What is prohibited

  • Spiked pickets and pointed tops that extend above the top rail — no sharpened points on any fence element
  • Decorative spikes or finials that could cause impalement injuries
  • Entrapment openings: Gaps between vertical elements must be either less than 3.5 inches or greater than 9 inches — the range between 3.5 and 9 inches can trap a child’s head

What is allowed

  • Flat-top pickets and fence boards
  • Dog-ear pickets (the standard residential fence picket)
  • Horizontal slat fences with proper spacing
  • Metal panel fences with openings under 3.5 inches or over 9 inches
  • Solid board-on-board privacy fences with no gaps

If you are replacing an existing fence that has pointed pickets, the new fence must comply with the safe fencing code. You cannot rebuild the same design.

HOA Requirements

Your HOA likely has stricter requirements than the city. Most Austin HOAs regulate:

CategoryCommon HOA Requirements
MaterialCedar or quality wood; no pine, chain link, or vinyl in visible areas
Height6 ft max (some allow 8 ft in rear)
Color / finishNatural cedar, specific stain colors, or paint to match community
StyleBoard-on-board, shadowbox, or solid picket; horizontal slat often restricted
Finished sideMust face outward (neighbor / street side)
Gate hardwareSelf-closing, self-latching (pool fence requirement applies HOA-wide in some communities)
SetbackMay require fence to be set back from property line (1–3 ft is common)

The approval process

  1. Get your property survey — most HOAs require a current survey showing the proposed fence location
  2. Submit an ACC application — the Architectural Control Committee reviews fence plans
  3. Include a site plan — show the fence location relative to property lines, structures, and easements
  4. Specify materials and finish — the ACC wants to know exactly what you are building
  5. Wait for written approval — do not start construction until you have approval in writing

Building without HOA approval can result in fines, daily penalties, or a removal order. The cost of removal and rebuilding is far more than the cost of getting approval first.

Property Line and Neighbor Issues

Building on the property line

You can build a fence on the property line, but every part of the fence — including post footings — must be on your property. If any part encroaches on your neighbor’s property, they can require removal. Before building:

  • Get a survey: $400–$800 and worth every dollar. Do not rely on existing stakes, old fences, or assumptions about where the line is
  • Set posts 2–4 inches inside your line: This provides a buffer for post diameter and eliminates disputes
  • Communicate with your neighbor: Not legally required, but it prevents conflicts

Shared fence costs

Texas is not a shared-fence state. Your neighbor is not legally required to share the cost of a fence on the property line. If you want a fence, you pay for it. If your neighbor wants to contribute, that is a private agreement between the two of you.

Fence facing

Austin city code does not require the “good side” (finished side) to face your neighbor. However, many HOAs do require it. Board-on-board fences solve this by looking the same from both sides.

The city does not restrict fence materials. Your HOA almost certainly does. Cedar is the default in most Austin neighborhoods. Metal fencing — both mild steel panels and ornamental iron — is increasingly common in modern designs, and most HOAs approve it when it looks architectural rather than industrial.

Easements and Utilities

Before building, check your property for easements — areas where utility companies or the city have the right to access underground lines.

  • Utility easements: You can build a fence across a utility easement, but the utility company can remove it without compensation if they need access. Use removable fence panels or gate sections over easements
  • Drainage easements: Fences in drainage easements may require city approval and must not impede water flow. Solid fences are often prohibited; open-style fences may be allowed
  • Call 811 before digging: Texas law requires you to call 811 at least 48 hours before digging fence post holes. Utility companies will mark underground lines for free

Fence Permit Checklist

  1. Check floodplain status at austintexas.gov
  2. Confirm fence height is under 7 feet (under 42 inches in front yard)
  3. Check corner lot visibility triangle if applicable
  4. Review HOA requirements and submit ACC application
  5. Get a property survey or confirm existing survey is current
  6. Check for easements on your plat
  7. Call 811 at least 48 hours before digging
  8. Ensure fence design complies with safe fencing code
  9. Communicate with neighbors about the project
