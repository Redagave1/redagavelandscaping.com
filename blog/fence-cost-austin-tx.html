How Much Does a Fence Cost in Austin, TX?

June 30, 2026

Whether you need privacy from neighbors, security for kids and pets, or a design element that frames your landscape, a fence is one of the most common home improvement projects in Austin. The price range is wide because fencing materials span from basic chain link to ornamental wrought iron — and your choice of material, height, and style determines the final cost.

The short answer: a fence in Austin costs $15 to $65 per linear foot fully installed. A standard 150-foot wood privacy fence runs $3,750 to $6,750. A cedar fence for the same length costs $4,500 to $7,500. The average Austin homeowner pays $3,500 to $5,000 for a complete residential fence installation.

Here is a full breakdown by material, what gates cost, Austin’s permit and height rules, and how to get the best value.

Cedar is the dominant fencing material in Austin for good reason — it resists insects naturally, weathers beautifully, and performs well in our climate. Steel provides the clean, modern lines that complement contemporary Austin architecture and requires zero maintenance.

Austin Fence Regulations

Austin has specific rules that affect every fence project. Know these before you choose a design:

Height Limits

  • 6 feet maximum for solid fences along property lines, measured from natural grade
  • 7 to 8 feet allowed in some situations depending on grade changes, setbacks, and adjacency to commercial or industrial uses
  • 2 to 3 feet maximum within visibility triangles on corner lots to maintain traffic sight lines
  • Any fence over 7 feet requires a building permit

Permit Requirements

  • Most standard 6-foot residential fences do not require a permit
  • A permit is required if the fence exceeds 7 feet at any point
  • A permit is required for fences over 6 feet along a public right-of-way (not including alleys)
  • A permit is required for any fence in a floodplain regardless of height
  • Permit fees: $25 to $40

HOA Rules

Texas law (Senate Bill 1588, 2021) prevents HOAs from banning perimeter fences or pool enclosures. However, HOAs can regulate fence materials, colors, height, and style. Many Austin HOAs require pre-approval before construction. Check your HOA guidelines first, but know they cannot prohibit a fence entirely.

Material Deep Dives

Cedar — $25 to $50 per linear foot

Cedar is Austin’s fence material of choice, and for good reason. Western red cedar contains natural oils that repel insects and resist rot without chemical treatment. It takes stain beautifully and, left untreated, weathers to an attractive silver-gray that many Austin homeowners prefer. Board-on-board construction — where pickets overlap with no gaps — provides complete privacy from any angle. Cedar fences last 15 to 20 years with periodic sealing, or 10 to 15 years left natural.

Pressure-Treated Wood — $25 to $35 per linear foot

The budget option for privacy fencing. Pressure-treated pine is chemically treated to resist rot and insects. It costs 20% to 40% less than cedar but does not look as good, does not weather as gracefully, and requires more frequent staining and sealing to avoid warping and cracking. In Austin’s intense sun, untreated pressure-treated wood grays and deteriorates quickly. It is the right choice when budget is the primary concern and you are willing to maintain it annually.

Steel — $35 to $65 per linear foot

Steel fencing provides the clean, modern lines that fit Austin’s contemporary architecture. Available in tubular panels, flat bar, or horizontal slat styles. Powder-coated steel requires zero maintenance and lasts 20 to 50 years. Corten steel develops a natural rust patina that complements Hill Country landscapes. Steel is the premium choice for front yard fencing, pool enclosures, and design-forward properties. Note: 2025 tariff increases raised metal fence prices 8% to 15%.

Vinyl — $30 to $60 per linear foot

The zero-maintenance option. Vinyl never needs painting, staining, or sealing. It will not rot, warp, or attract termites. It lasts 20 to 30 years with nothing more than an occasional rinse. The higher upfront cost versus wood pays for itself by year 5 to 7 when you factor in eliminated staining costs. The downside: vinyl has a manufactured look that some homeowners find less attractive than natural wood, and it offers limited color and style options compared to cedar.

Chain Link — $15 to $35 per linear foot

The most affordable option by far. Chain link is functional rather than decorative — it works for pet containment, property boundaries, and large perimeters where privacy is not the goal. Privacy slats can be added for $3 to $5 per linear foot to block some visibility. Vinyl-coated chain link (black or green) looks better than galvanized silver and costs $2 to $5 more per foot. Chain link lasts 15 to 20 years with minimal maintenance.

Austin-Specific Cost Factors

  • Rocky soil: Austin sits on limestone. Rocky soil requires additional labor and equipment to set posts, adding $500 to $2,000 to a project. Some areas need jackhammering for post holes
  • Tree roots: Austin’s heritage tree ordinance protects trees with trunks 19 inches or larger in diameter. Fence lines may need to route around protected root zones, adding material and labor
  • Slope and grade: Fence installation on sloped terrain requires stepped or racked panels. This adds 10% to 25% to the project cost compared to flat installations
  • Old fence removal: Removing an existing fence costs $3 to $5 per linear foot for wood and $5 to $8 for chain link, including haul-away
  • Property survey: If you do not know your exact property lines, a survey costs $300 to $700. Installing a fence on the wrong side of the property line is an expensive mistake

Repair vs. Replace

Not every fence problem requires full replacement. Here is how to decide:

  • Replace if more than 20% of pickets or posts are damaged, posts are rotting at the base, or the fence is leaning significantly
  • Repair if damage is limited to a few boards, one or two posts, or cosmetic issues like fading
  • Fence repair cost: $200 to $800 for minor work (a few boards, one post), $800 to $2,000 for major repairs (multiple posts, long sections)
  • Rule of thumb: if repair cost exceeds 50% of replacement cost, replace the entire fence
