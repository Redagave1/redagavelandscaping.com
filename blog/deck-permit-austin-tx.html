Do You Need a Deck Permit in Austin, TX?

July 3, 2026

Most residential decks in Austin require a building permit. The exemption is narrow: a small, low, freestanding platform that is not attached to the house and does not provide egress. If you are building a deck worth building — attached to your home, elevated for a view, or large enough for a dining table — you need a permit. Here is the full guide, including the impervious cover rules that make Austin’s deck permitting more complex than most Texas cities.

When You Do Not Need a Permit

A deck is exempt from a building permit in Austin only if it meets all five of these conditions:

  1. No more than 200 square feet in area
  2. No more than 30 inches above grade at any point
  3. Not attached to the dwelling
  4. Does not serve as required egress from the building
  5. Not in a flood hazard area

If your deck fails any one of these conditions, you need a permit. A 10×20 ground-level platform that is freestanding and under 30 inches? Exempt. The same platform attached to your house with a ledger board? Permit required.

The 30-Inch Rule

Thirty inches is the magic number in Austin’s deck code. It divides decks into two categories:

Under 30 inches above grade

  • No guardrails required
  • Simpler structural requirements
  • May qualify for permit exemption (if all other conditions are met)
  • Faster review if a permit is needed

Over 30 inches above grade

  • Guardrails required — minimum 36 inches tall
  • Baluster spacing — a 4-inch sphere cannot pass through (max gap of approximately 3.5 inches)
  • Structural load requirements — railings must withstand 200 pounds of concentrated force at any point on the top rail
  • Foundation requirements — footings must be designed for the load and soil conditions
  • Structural plans required for permit application
  • Inspections required during and after construction

Height is measured from the lowest point of grade directly below the deck surface to the top of the deck boards. If your yard slopes and the deck is 24 inches above grade on one side but 36 inches on the other, it is classified as an over-30-inch deck.

Impervious Cover: Austin’s Deck Advantage

Here is where decks get interesting in Austin. Unlike concrete patios that count at 100% impervious cover, an uncovered wood or composite deck with drainage gaps between boards, built over a pervious surface, counts at only 50% of its area.

How different surfaces count

SurfaceImpervious Cover %400 sq ft = IC Impact
Poured concrete patio100%400 sq ft
Paver patio100% (standard pavers)400 sq ft
Permeable pavers (certified)0–50%0–200 sq ft
Uncovered deck with gaps over pervious ground50%200 sq ft
Deck over existing concrete100%400 sq ft
Covered deck (solid roof)100%400 sq ft

On a lot near its impervious cover limit, building an uncovered deck instead of a concrete patio gives you twice the outdoor living space for the same impervious cover impact. This is one reason elevated IPE and composite decks are popular in Austin — they expand your usable outdoor area without maxing out your impervious cover allowance.

Austin’s building code requires all exposed wood in deck construction to be naturally decay-resistant or pressure-treated.

All structural members that contact the ground or are within 6 inches of grade must be ground-contact rated (UC4A or UC4B treatment level). This applies to posts, beams, and any joists near grade. Red Agave builds deck frames in mild steel — not wood — which eliminates the wood decay concern for structural components entirely. The steel frame is powder coated, and the decking material (IPE or composite) sits on top.

Stairs and landings that project beyond the deck footprint must also respect setback requirements. If your deck is 6 feet from the rear property line and the stairs project 4 feet, you have only 2 feet of clearance — which may violate the setback.

The Permit Process

What to submit

  1. Site plan: Deck location, dimensions, distance to property lines, and existing structures
  2. Structural plans: Foundation type and size, post/beam/joist sizes and spacing, connection details, railing details
  3. Impervious cover calculation: Current and proposed totals
  4. Ledger board detail: How the deck connects to the house (attached decks only)
  5. Tree survey: If protected trees (≥19″ diameter) are in the construction zone

Timeline and cost

ItemTimeline / Cost
Permit application preparation1–2 weeks
City plan review5–10 business days
Corrections (if needed)3–5 business days per round
Permit fee$300–$800 (based on project value)
Inspections (foundation, framing, final)Scheduled during construction

Required inspections

  1. Foundation inspection: After footings are poured or piers are set, before framing begins
  2. Framing inspection: After the structural frame is complete, before decking is installed
  3. Final inspection: After all decking, railings, stairs, and finishes are complete

Each inspection must be scheduled at least 24 hours in advance. If an inspection fails, corrections must be made and the inspection rescheduled.

These ranges include the permit fee, structural plans, and all inspections. Foundation costs vary significantly based on height — a ground-level deck on concrete piers costs far less than a 6-foot elevated deck requiring engineered footings on Austin’s clay soil.

Deck Permit Checklist

  1. Determine if your deck is exempt (all 5 conditions must be met)
  2. Calculate impervious cover (current + proposed)
  3. Confirm setback requirements for your zoning
  4. Check floodplain status
  5. Identify protected trees in the construction area
  6. Prepare structural plans (engineer-sealed for complex projects)
  7. Submit HOA application if applicable
  8. Submit city permit application
  9. Schedule inspections during construction
  10. Get final inspection approval before use
