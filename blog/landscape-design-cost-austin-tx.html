How Much Does Landscape Design Cost in Austin, TX?

June 30, 2026

A professional landscape design is the difference between a yard that looks assembled at random and one that feels intentional — where every material, plant, and structure works together. But design costs vary dramatically depending on who you hire, how complex your property is, and how detailed you need the plans to be.

The short answer: landscape design in Austin costs $500 to $8,000+ depending on scope and the professional you hire. A basic planting plan costs $400 to $800. A comprehensive design-build package with hardscape plans, planting design, grading, and construction documents costs $4,000 to $8,000+. The average Austin homeowner spends $1,500 to $4,000 on landscape design.

Here is a full breakdown of pricing models, what you get at each level, designer vs. architect differences, and when the design-build model gives you the best value.

Design Cost by Scope

Pricing Models

Hourly Rate

Landscape designers charge $50 to $150 per hour. Landscape architects charge $100 to $250 per hour. Hourly billing works well for consultations and small projects where the scope is hard to define upfront. The risk: costs can escalate if the design process takes longer than expected. Always ask for a time estimate and cap before starting hourly work.

Flat Fee

A flat fee for a complete design package is the most common pricing model in Austin. You know the total cost before starting. Fees range from $500 for a basic plan to $8,000+ for a comprehensive design-build package. Flat fees are typically based on property size, project complexity, and the level of detail in the deliverables. Most flat-fee designs include 1 to 2 rounds of revisions, with additional revisions billed hourly.

Percentage of Project Cost

For large or complex projects, some designers charge 10% to 20% of the total installation budget. This model aligns the designer’s effort with the project’s complexity — a $100,000 project requires more detailed plans than a $20,000 project. A 15% design fee on a $50,000 landscape project is $7,500. This model is common for landscape architects managing the entire project from design through construction oversight.

When you need a landscape architect: projects involving grading changes, drainage engineering, retaining walls over 4 feet, structures requiring permits, or plans that need a licensed professional’s stamp. In Austin, this includes most hillside properties, any project in a floodplain, and properties with significant drainage challenges from our clay soil.

When a landscape designer is sufficient: planting design, garden layout, material selection for patios and walkways, and aesthetic vision for properties without significant engineering challenges.

The Design-Build Advantage

A design-build firm handles both design and construction under one contract. This model has significant advantages:

  • Design fee credit: Many design-build firms charge a separate design fee ($500 to $5,000) that is credited toward the construction contract if you hire them to build. Effectively, you get the design for free
  • Cost accuracy: The designer who creates the plans also builds them, so cost estimates are realistic — not theoretical. No surprise budget gaps when the design goes to bid
  • Continuity: The builder understands every design intent because they were part of creating it. No interpretation errors or change orders from miscommunication
  • Accountability: One firm is responsible for the entire result. No finger-pointing between designer and builder when something does not match the plan

The downside of design-build: you are committing to one firm before seeing competitive bids from other builders. If budget competition is important to you, a standalone design that you can bid to multiple builders preserves that option.

What a Design Should Include

Site Analysis

A thorough evaluation of existing conditions: sun exposure patterns, soil type and drainage, existing trees and structures, views to preserve or screen, grade changes, utility locations, and any site challenges. In Austin, this should include clay soil assessment and drainage evaluation.

Concept Plan

An overhead layout showing the spatial organization of the entire property: where the patio goes, how pathways connect, where planting beds are located, how the outdoor living spaces relate to each other and to the house. This is the big-picture vision.

Planting Plan

Species selection, sizes at planting, mature sizes, spacing, quantities, and placement. A good planting plan accounts for Austin’s heat, drought tolerance, soil pH (our alkaline clay), deer resistance in Hill Country areas, and seasonal interest across all four seasons.

Hardscape Plans

Materials, dimensions, patterns, and construction details for patios, walkways, retaining walls, decks, fencing, outdoor kitchens, and other structures.

Grading and Drainage

How the property will be shaped to manage water. Critical in Austin where clay soil creates chronic drainage problems. Includes spot elevations, slope directions, and drainage infrastructure locations.

Lighting and Irrigation

Landscape lighting fixture locations, zones, and control strategy. Irrigation zones, head types, and coverage areas. Both systems should be designed before construction begins to avoid retrofit costs.

Timeline

  • Basic planting plan: 1 to 2 weeks
  • Mid-level design: 2 to 4 weeks
  • Comprehensive design-build package: 4 to 8 weeks
  • Complex projects with engineering and permits: 8 to 12 weeks

These timelines include the initial site visit, concept development, client presentations, revisions, and final plan delivery. The biggest variable is client decision-making — the faster you provide feedback and approve concepts, the faster the process moves.

Is It Worth It?

A $2,000 design for a $50,000 landscape project is 4% of the total cost. That investment prevents costly mistakes: wrong plant selections that die and need replacement, drainage problems that damage foundations, hardscape layouts that do not function well for how you actually live, and change orders during construction that cost 3 to 5 times what they would have cost to get right in the design phase.

The design also ensures logical phasing if you plan to build in stages. Without a master plan, earlier phases often need to be partially demolished to accommodate later work — wasted money and wasted time.
