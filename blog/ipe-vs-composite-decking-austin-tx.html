IPE vs Composite Decking in Austin, TX

July 2, 2026

IPE and composite are the two serious decking materials for Austin. Pressure-treated pine rots. Cedar warps. Those are fine in mild climates, but Austin’s UV, heat cycles, and summer storms destroy softwood decks within 5–8 years. The real decision is between a natural hardwood that lasts 50–75 years and a manufactured composite that lasts 25–30 years. Here is the honest comparison, with specific numbers for Austin’s climate.

The Heat Problem

This is the Austin-specific factor that most national comparisons miss. In July and August, deck surfaces in direct sun can reach dangerous temperatures. How each material performs:

MaterialSurface Temp (direct sun, July)Barefoot Comfort
IPE (oiled, medium brown)120–140°FHot but tolerable briefly
IPE (silver patina)115–135°FBetter — lighter color
Composite (dark color)150–170°FToo hot for bare feet
Composite (light color)130–150°FHot — brief contact only
Premium composite (AZEK)120–140°FComparable to IPE

IPE stays 10–15 degrees cooler than standard composite in the same sun exposure because natural wood has lower thermal mass than the plastic-and-wood-fiber mixture in composite boards. If you are building a pool deck or an unshaded dining area where people walk barefoot, this temperature difference matters.

The solution for either material: add a shade structure. A steel pergola or pavilion over the deck drops the surface temperature 20–30 degrees and makes any decking material comfortable.

Appearance Over Time

IPE: Year 1 through Year 30

  • Year 1: Rich, dark brown with deep grain pattern. Stunning
  • Year 2–3: If oiled annually, maintains original color. If left natural, begins weathering to silver-gray
  • Year 5: Oiled: still rich brown. Natural: uniform silver patina
  • Year 10: Either finish looks intentional and aged. Surface still smooth and hard
  • Year 20: Silver patina has the look of a boardwalk or marine dock — a character that only real wood develops
  • Year 30: Can be sanded back to bare wood and re-oiled to look nearly new. No other decking material offers this

Composite: Year 1 through Year 25

  • Year 1: Uniform color, consistent texture. Clean and even
  • Year 2–3: Some initial fading (normal, covered by warranty). Mold may appear in grooved boards in shaded areas
  • Year 5: Color has settled. Some staining from grills, planters, or furniture may be visible
  • Year 10: Noticeable fading compared to a new board. Surface scratches from furniture visible in certain light
  • Year 15: Boards beginning to show wear. Some cupping or warping possible in extreme heat
  • Year 25: End of warranty period. No refinishing option — replace entire deck or live with the appearance

Maintenance: What Each Actually Requires

IPE annual maintenance

  • Required: Sweep debris. Hose down quarterly. That is it
  • Optional: Apply IPE oil once per year (spring) to maintain the brown color. This takes a few hours for a 400 sq ft deck. If you skip oiling, the wood weathers to silver — it does not deteriorate, it just changes color
  • Every 5–10 years: Light sanding of any surface areas that feel rough (rare with IPE due to its density)

Composite annual maintenance

  • Required: Clean with mild soap and water 2–3 times per year. Clear debris from grooved board channels to prevent mold growth
  • Required: Remove stains from food, drink, or grease immediately — composite absorbs stains that IPE does not
  • Every 1–2 years: Inspect for mold in shaded areas and treat with composite-specific cleaner
  • Never: Sand, stain, paint, or seal composite. These damage the capped surface

Composite is marketed as “maintenance-free,” but in Austin’s humid conditions, mold prevention and stain management are real ongoing tasks. IPE’s only optional maintenance — annual oiling — is a choice about color, not structural necessity.

When to Choose IPE

  • You are building a deck you plan to keep for 20+ years
  • You want a natural material that ages with character
  • You are building a pool deck where barefoot heat matters
  • You want the option to refinish the surface years from now
  • You are building an outdoor kitchen where the deck is the primary living surface
  • You value the look and feel of real hardwood over manufactured uniformity
  • You are pairing the deck with mild steel elements (pergola, railing, planters) and want materials that age together

When to Choose Composite

  • Budget is the primary constraint and you need to minimize upfront cost
  • You want guaranteed uniform color and do not want to oil annually
  • The deck is in full shade (heat differential is less significant)
  • You are building a secondary deck or utility area (mudroom exit, side yard platform) where material character is less important
  • You plan to sell the home within 10 years and want the deck to look new at listing

What About Steel Framing?

Regardless of which decking material you choose, consider a steel substructure instead of pressure-treated lumber. A mild steel frame will outlast both decking surfaces — when the composite boards reach end of life in 25 years, you can replace just the boards without rebuilding the frame. With IPE on a steel frame, the entire structure lasts 50+ years with zero substructure concerns.

Red Agave builds deck frames in mild steel — not aluminum, not wood. Steel framing pairs with both IPE and composite and eliminates the most common failure point in deck construction: the substructure rotting before the surface.

Our Recommendation for Austin

We recommend IPE for primary outdoor living decks — the deck you eat dinner on, the deck around your pool, the deck under your pergola. It stays cooler, lasts longer, looks better with age, and can be restored. We build it on mild steel frames with hidden fasteners and cable railing for a clean, modern profile.

For secondary areas where budget matters more than material character, composite is a practical choice. But even then, we use a steel frame underneath and premium-line composite (not builder-grade) to ensure the deck performs in Austin’s heat.
