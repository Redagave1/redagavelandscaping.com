Fall Landscaping in Austin, TX — The Complete Guide

July 3, 2026

Fall is not downtime in Austin landscaping — it is the most important season of the year. The soil is still warm enough for root growth, air temperatures are dropping out of the 100°F range, and water restrictions ease as demand falls. Every tree you plant, every bed you mulch, and every irrigation repair you make in October pays dividends for years. Here is the complete fall checklist.

Aeration

Austin’s clay soil compacts hard over summer. Core aeration — pulling 2–3 inch plugs with a hollow-tine aerator — is the most effective treatment. It increases water absorption by up to 25%, reduces runoff, and opens channels for fertilizer and oxygen to reach the root zone.

When and how to aerate

  • Window: Late September through mid-October. Soil temperature should be above 65°F
  • Soil moisture: Water the day before. The aerator needs soft soil to pull clean plugs — dry clay soil breaks the tines
  • Core vs. spike: Core aeration only. Spike aerators push clay sideways, making compaction worse below the surface
  • Pattern: Two passes in perpendicular directions. Leave plugs on the surface — they break down within 2 weeks and return organic matter to the soil
  • After aeration: Apply fertilizer or overseed immediately — the open cores give amendments direct access to the root zone

Do not apply nitrogen after mid-September. Late nitrogen pushes soft leaf growth that is vulnerable to the first freeze. Potassium, on the other hand, strengthens cell walls, improves drought recovery, and helps turf survive cold snaps. The winterizer application in late October is the most important fertilizer of the year.

Tree and Shrub Planting

Fall is the best time to plant trees in Austin — the soil is warm enough for root establishment while cool air reduces water demand. Trees planted in October–November develop 3–4 months of root growth before summer heat arrives.

Planting guidelines

  • Dig the hole 2–3 times wider than the root ball but no deeper
  • Set the root flare at or slightly above grade — burying the flare is the number one cause of tree decline
  • Backfill with native soil, not amended soil — amended holes create a “bathtub effect” where roots refuse to grow into the surrounding clay
  • Water deeply once per week through the first winter — do not rely on rainfall
  • Mulch 3–4 inches in a wide ring around the tree, keeping mulch 4 inches from the trunk

Overseeding Bermuda with Ryegrass

Bermuda goes dormant and turns brown after the first frost (average November 28 in Austin). Overseeding with perennial ryegrass gives you green color through winter.

How to overseed

  1. Timing: Mid-October, when nighttime temperatures drop below 65°F consistently
  2. Mow low: Scalp the Bermuda to 1 inch so ryegrass seed reaches the soil
  3. Aerate first: Core aeration creates seed-to-soil contact that dramatically improves germination
  4. Seed rate: 8–10 pounds of perennial ryegrass per 1,000 sq ft
  5. Water: Keep soil moist for 10–14 days until germination (light, frequent watering — this is the exception to the “deep and infrequent” rule)
  6. First mow: Mow at 2 inches when ryegrass reaches 3 inches

The ryegrass dies naturally in late spring as temperatures rise above 85°F, and your Bermuda comes back underneath. The downside is cost — seed, extra watering, and a slightly delayed Bermuda green-up in spring.

Irrigation Adjustments

Fall watering mistakes are as common as summer ones, just in the opposite direction.

MonthFrequencyRun Time vs. SummerNotes
SeptemberOnce per weekSame as summerStill hot — do not reduce yet
OctoberOnce per weekReduce 20–30%Cooler temps, less evaporation
NovemberEvery 10–14 daysReduce 40–50%Watch for overwatering — cool soil holds moisture
DecemberEvery 2–3 weeksReduce 60–70%Only water if no rain for 2+ weeks

The biggest fall mistake: Running the same irrigation schedule from summer into fall. Cool soil holds moisture much longer — overwatering in fall creates root rot, fungal disease (take all patch, brown patch), and suffocates roots that need oxygen to establish.

Hardscape Projects for Fall

Fall is the ideal season for construction. Crews work in comfortable temperatures, concrete cures evenly, and you get to enjoy the finished project through the holidays. Projects to tackle in fall:

  • Patios — concrete cures best in 50–70°F temperatures, not summer heat
  • Pergolas — mild steel fabrication and installation run year-round
  • Outdoor kitchens — build in fall, enjoy through holiday entertaining season
  • Fire pits — the ultimate fall installation — build it and use it the same month
  • Landscape lighting — shorter days mean more hours to enjoy lighting
  • Steel edging — define beds before spring planting
  • Retaining walls — address drainage issues before winter rains

Leaf Management

Austin’s live oaks drop leaves in March, not fall — but red oaks, pecans, and other deciduous trees shed from November through January. How to handle it:

  • Mulch in place: Use a mulching mower to shred leaves into the lawn. A thin layer of shredded leaves adds organic matter and nitrogen
  • Remove heavy layers: A thick mat of whole leaves blocks sunlight and traps moisture, promoting fungal disease. If you cannot see grass through the leaves, remove or mulch them
  • Compost: Bag extra leaves and compost them for use in spring beds
  • Keep beds covered: Leaves in landscape beds can stay — they act as natural mulch

Preparing for the First Freeze

Austin’s average first frost is November 28, but freezes have arrived as early as late October. Be ready:

  1. Protect tropicals: Cover plumeria, hibiscus, citrus, and bougainvillea with frost cloth when a freeze is forecast. Move potted tropicals indoors or against the house
  2. Disconnect hoses: Water in a disconnected hose drains out. Water in a connected hose can freeze, expand, and crack the spigot
  3. Insulate backflow preventers: Wrap outdoor irrigation backflow devices with foam insulation
  4. Last mow: Lower St. Augustine to 3 inches and Bermuda to 1–1.5 inches for the final mow before dormancy. Shorter grass reduces fungal habitat over winter
  5. Water before a freeze: Moist soil holds heat better than dry soil. Water deeply 24–48 hours before a hard freeze to insulate roots

The Fall Advantage

Everything you do in fall compounds. A tree planted in November has four months of root growth before summer. Beds mulched in October stay insulated through winter and retain moisture into spring. Hardscape built in fall is ready for spring entertaining. Fall is when smart Austin landscapes are made — summer just reveals whether you did the work.
