How Much Does a Deck Cost in Austin, TX?

June 30, 2026

A well-built deck is one of the highest-ROI outdoor improvements you can make to an Austin home. The climate supports year-round outdoor living, and a deck that extends your usable space pays for itself in lifestyle and resale value. But the price range is enormous — a basic ground-level platform and a multi-level hardwood entertaining deck are completely different projects.

The short answer: building a deck in Austin costs $25 to $80 per square foot fully installed. A standard 200 square foot deck with mid-range composite decking runs $8,000 to $14,000. A 300 square foot raised composite deck costs $13,000 to $19,000. A large 500 square foot IPE hardwood deck with stairs, railings, and lighting can run $30,000 to $45,000.

Here is a full breakdown by material, size, features, and what Austin’s climate means for your material choice.

Cost Per Square Foot by Material

Material is the single biggest cost driver. The range from budget to premium is roughly 3x, and the right choice depends on your budget, maintenance tolerance, and how long you plan to stay in the home:

MaterialInstalled Cost / sq ft
Pressure-treated pine — Most affordable. Requires annual staining and sealing in Austin’s climate. Lifespan 10–15 years for the deck surface$25 – $40
Cedar — Natural insect and rot resistance. Attractive grain. Needs annual sealing. Lifespan 15–20 years$30 – $50
Mid-grade composite — Brands like Trex Enhance or Select. No staining or sealing. 25–30 year warranty$35 – $55
Premium composite — Trex Transcend, TimberTech. UV-resistant cap layer. 50-year warranty. Best for Austin sun$50 – $70
Capped cellular PVC — Fully synthetic. Lightest weight, highest moisture resistance. Limited-lifetime warranty$55 – $80
IPE hardwood — Brazilian hardwood. Incredibly dense (sinks in water). 40–75 year lifespan. Our specialty$55 – $80

Our recommendation for Austin: premium capped composite or IPE hardwood. Pressure-treated pine is tempting because of the low upfront cost, but Austin’s intense UV and heat cycles cause it to crack, warp, and gray within 2 to 3 years without diligent annual maintenance. Most homeowners underestimate the ongoing cost and effort. Composite and IPE have higher upfront costs but virtually zero maintenance and dramatically longer lifespans.

Total Project Cost by Size

Here is what complete deck projects cost in Austin at various sizes, using mid-range composite decking with standard railings:

ProjectTotal Cost
100 sq ft ground-level (10’ × 10’) — small platform, no railings$3,000 – $6,000
200 sq ft standard (12’ × 16’) — entertaining deck with railings$8,000 – $14,000
300 sq ft raised (15’ × 20’) — elevated 24”+, stairs and railings$13,000 – $19,000
400 sq ft large (20’ × 20’) — multi-level or wraparound$18,000 – $30,000
500 sq ft+ premium — IPE or premium composite, full features$30,000 – $45,000+

Elevated decks cost 30% to 50% more than ground-level decks because of the additional substructure — posts, beams, joists, and hardware needed to support the deck above grade. A second-story deck attached to an upper floor can add 50% to 100% to the base price.

Feature Add-Ons and Their Costs

The base deck price covers the platform, substructure, and basic finishing. Most projects include additional features that add to the total:

FeatureCost
Railings (wood) — Required if deck is 30”+ above grade. Per linear foot$20 – $60 / lf
Railings (composite) — Matches deck material. No maintenance. Per linear foot$35 – $80 / lf
Railings (cable or glass) — Modern aesthetic. Premium option. Per linear foot$60 – $210 / lf
Stairs — Standard set of 8–16 steps with wood stringers$1,400 – $2,600
Stairs (metal stringers) — Premium, longer-lasting$2,000 – $3,500
Built-in bench seating — Custom framed and capped$1,000 – $3,000
Pergola — Partial shade structure over deck. Per square foot of coverage$10 – $45 / sq ft
Solid roof cover — Full shade and rain protection. Per square foot$60 – $155 / sq ft
Deck lighting — Post caps, stair risers, rail lights. Per fixture installed$30 – $150 each
Skirting / lattice — Conceals under-deck on raised decks$5 – $15 / lf

A 200 square foot deck with composite railings on three sides (40 linear feet), one set of stairs, and 6 deck lights adds roughly $3,500 to $6,000 to the base deck cost. A pergola over the deck adds $2,000 to $9,000 depending on size and material.

Labor vs. Materials: Where the Money Goes

Labor is the largest single cost in most deck projects, accounting for 50% to 60% of the total. Here is how the budget typically breaks down:

  • Labor: $15 to $45 per square foot, depending on crew experience, deck complexity, and material. IPE is the most labor-intensive because the wood is extremely dense and requires pre-drilling for every fastener. Composite is lighter and faster to install
  • Substructure (posts, beams, joists, hardware): 30% to 38% of total project cost. This is the same regardless of your decking material choice
  • Decking boards and fasteners: 20% to 30% of total cost. This is where material choice has the biggest impact
  • Railings: 10% to 15% of total cost if included

Labor rates for skilled carpenters in Central Texas rose 12% between 2023 and 2025, and rates continue to climb. This is a market where you get what you pay for — the cheapest bid often means the least experienced crew, and substructure quality is not something you can inspect once the deck boards are down.

Austin Permits and Building Code

Not every deck needs a permit. In Austin, a deck is exempt from permits if it meets all of these criteria:

  • Not attached to the dwelling
  • 200 square feet or less
  • 30 inches or less above grade at any point
  • Does not serve as required egress from the building
  • Not located in a flood hazard area

If your deck does not meet all five criteria, you need a building permit from the City of Austin Development Services Department. Most attached decks and any deck over 30 inches high require a permit. Permit fees typically run $300 to $1,500 depending on project scope, and the permit process adds 2 to 6 weeks to your timeline.

Austin building code requires guards (railings) not less than 36 inches high on any deck surface more than 30 inches above grade. The city adopted the 2024 International Residential Code with local amendments, effective July 2025. Your contractor should handle the full permit process.

Why Material Choice Matters More in Austin

Austin’s climate is harder on deck materials than most cities. The combination of factors that affect your deck here:

  • UV intensity: Austin gets intense, sustained UV exposure from April through October. Untreated wood grays and degrades on the surface within months. This is not just cosmetic — UV breakdown opens the wood to moisture penetration that accelerates rot and cracking
  • Heat cycling: Deck surfaces regularly exceed 130°F in direct sun. The daily expansion and contraction cycle from 70°F mornings to 130°F+ afternoons causes wood to crack, split, and warp over time. Pressure-treated pine is especially vulnerable
  • Sudden storms: Austin gets heavy, fast downpours followed by intense sun. This wet-dry cycling is the worst possible pattern for untreated wood. It swells when wet, shrinks when dry, and the stress fractures the grain
  • Humidity and mold: Spring and fall humidity supports mold and mildew growth on wood surfaces, especially in shaded areas. Under-deck moisture traps make this worse on elevated decks without proper ventilation

The result: pressure-treated wood that might last 15 to 20 years in a temperate climate often needs board replacement within 8 to 12 years in Austin if not meticulously maintained. Composite and IPE do not have these problems.

Cedar$9,000 – $15,000$400 – $800$13,000 – $23,000
Mid-grade composite$10,500 – $16,500$100 – $300$11,500 – $19,500
Premium composite$15,000 – $21,000$100 – $200$16,000 – $23,000

Mid-grade composite has the lowest total cost of ownership over 10 years. Pressure-treated pine — the cheapest upfront — is actually the most expensive over time when you factor in annual staining ($550 to $1,250), sealing ($550 to $1,330), and board replacement. IPE costs the most upfront but lasts 40 to 75 years, making it the cheapest option per year of use.

ROI: Does a Deck Add Value to Your Home?

Decks consistently rank among the top exterior improvements for return on investment:

  • Wood deck ROI: 83% to 95% of cost recouped at resale (Zonda 2025 Cost vs. Value Report)
  • Composite deck ROI: 68% to 89% of cost recouped at resale
  • National average (wood, 16’ × 20’): $18,263 cost, $17,350 recouped at sale
  • National average (composite, 16’ × 20’): $25,096 cost, $22,200 recouped at sale

ROI is highest in markets with strong outdoor living cultures — which describes Austin perfectly. A well-designed deck that extends living space, complements the home’s architecture, and includes features like lighting and shade is viewed as a lifestyle upgrade by Austin buyers, not just a commodity addition.

Covered vs. Uncovered: The Shade Question

In Austin, shade is not optional for a deck you actually want to use during summer. An uncovered deck in direct afternoon sun can hit 130°F+ on the surface, making it unusable from noon to 6 PM for four months of the year. Your options:

  • Pergola: $10 to $45 per square foot. Provides partial, filtered shade. Enough to make the deck usable. Can support climbing plants for additional coverage. Most popular option in Austin
  • Shade sail: $2,000 to $5,000 installed. Removable, affordable, but less permanent and less attractive
  • Solid roof extension: $60 to $155 per square foot. Full shade and rain protection. Matches the home’s roofline. Significantly increases usable days per year but adds substantial cost

If you are building a deck primarily for entertaining, a pergola over at least part of the deck is the best value-to-comfort trade-off in this climate.

Saving Money on Your Deck Project

Strategies that actually reduce deck cost without sacrificing quality:

  • Build during off-season: Many Austin contractors offer 10% to 20% discounts during winter when demand drops. December through February is the sweet spot
  • Keep the shape simple: Rectangular decks minimize material waste and labor. Every angle, curve, and notch adds cutting time and scrap
  • Go ground-level: Eliminating the raised substructure saves 30% to 50% compared to an elevated deck. If your yard grade allows it, build at grade
  • Stay under 200 square feet and freestanding: This avoids the permit process entirely, saving $300 to $1,500 in fees and 2 to 6 weeks of wait time
  • Skip railings where code allows: Decks under 30 inches above grade do not require railings. That saves $20 to $100+ per linear foot
  • Phase the project: Build the deck now, add the pergola or lighting later. The deck is the structural investment — features can be added any time
