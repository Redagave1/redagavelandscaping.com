Gas vs Wood Fire Pit in Austin, TX

July 2, 2026

The gas vs wood fire pit question has a different answer in Austin than in most cities. Travis County issues burn bans regularly during dry conditions, and the City of Austin prohibits open burning. A wood-burning fire pit that you can only use half the year is not a backyard feature — it is a decoration. Here is the honest comparison, starting with the regulation that changes the entire conversation.

Austin Burn Ban Reality

This is the factor that most comparison articles do not address because they are written for a national audience. In Austin:

  • City of Austin: Open burning is prohibited. Wood-burning fire pits are only allowed if the fire is contained in a commercially manufactured device with a spark arrestor screen, and must comply with distance requirements from structures and property lines
  • Travis County burn bans: During drought conditions (common in summer and fall), Travis County issues burn bans that restrict all outdoor burning — including contained wood-burning fire pits. Violations carry fines
  • Gas fire pits: Generally exempt from burn bans because they produce a controlled flame with no flying sparks, embers, or significant particulate emissions

Travis County has issued burn bans in 7 of the last 15 years. When a burn ban is active, your wood-burning fire pit is off-limits. Your gas fire pit works every single night. For a feature you are investing $3,000–$20,000 in, reliability matters.

Our recommendation: Natural gas for any permanent, built-in fire feature. The gas line adds $500–$2,000 to the project but eliminates the ongoing hassle and cost of propane tank refills. If your fire pit is 50+ feet from the house or you do not have gas service, propane is the practical choice.

Gas Line Installation

Running a natural gas line to your fire pit requires a licensed plumber, a permit, and an inspection. Here is what to expect:

  • Trench: $15–$25 per linear foot for trenching and pipe installation from the gas meter to the fire pit location
  • Typical distance: 30–60 feet from the house, so $450–$1,500 for the trench and pipe
  • Shut-off valve: A dedicated shut-off valve at the fire pit ($100–$200)
  • Permit and inspection: $100–$300 depending on jurisdiction
  • Total gas line cost: $650–$2,000 depending on distance and complexity

Plan the gas line before the hardscape is installed. Running a gas line under an existing patio requires cutting and repairing the surface, which adds cost and disruption. The gas line should be part of the initial landscape design, not an afterthought.

The Case for Wood (When It Works)

Wood-burning fire pits are not wrong — they are limited in Austin. If you understand the limitations, a wood fire pit can be the right choice in specific situations:

  • Rural properties outside city limits where burn restrictions are less frequent and enforcement is different
  • Properties with large setbacks (fire pit 25+ feet from any structure) where the fire can be safely contained
  • Homeowners who value the experience of building a fire, the smell of wood smoke, and the sound of crackling logs
  • Cooking-focused fire features where you want coals for grilling, roasting, and wood-fired cooking

If you build a wood-burning fire pit in Austin, use a commercially manufactured fire pit with a spark arrestor screen, maintain 10+ feet of clearance from any structure or combustible material, keep a garden hose within reach, and never burn during a Travis County burn ban.
