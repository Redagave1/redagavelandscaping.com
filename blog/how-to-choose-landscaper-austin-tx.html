How to Choose a Landscaper in Austin, TX

July 1, 2026

Hiring the wrong landscaper is expensive. Not because their bid was high — usually the opposite. The real cost is paying someone else to fix bad drainage, pull up a poorly built patio, replace dead plants that were wrong for Austin’s clay soil, or tear out a retaining wall that was not engineered for the grade. Doing it twice always costs more than doing it right once.

Austin has hundreds of landscaping companies, from solo operators with a truck and a mower to full design-build firms with in-house designers and dedicated install crews. This guide will help you figure out which type you need, what to ask, what to look for in an estimate, and which red flags should send you to the next contractor on your list.

Step 1: Know What You Need

The first decision is what type of work you actually need. This determines the type of contractor to hire.

Project TypeWho to Hire
Lawn mowing, edging, leaf cleanup, basic maintenanceLawn care / maintenance company
New planting beds, mulch, seasonal color, small plant installationsLandscaper or garden center installer
Irrigation installation or repairLicensed irrigator (TCEQ licensed in TX)
Tree removal, major pruning, or diagnosisISA-certified arborist
Patio, retaining wall, outdoor kitchen, fire pit, drainage, full yard renovationDesign-build landscape contractor
Landscape plan only (no installation)Landscape designer or landscape architect

The biggest mistake homeowners make is hiring a lawn maintenance crew for a hardscape project, or hiring a hardscape contractor for a planting-only project. Match the contractor to the work.

Step 2: Design-Build vs. Separate Designer and Installer

For projects over $10,000 that include both hardscape and planting, you have two approaches:

Design-build (one company does everything)

  • The designer works with the install crew — what gets drawn is what gets built
  • Single point of accountability. If something goes wrong, there is no finger-pointing between designer and installer
  • Changes during construction are easier because the designer is in-house
  • Usually faster because there is no gap between design completion and construction start
  • The design fee is often folded into the project cost if you proceed with installation

Separate designer and installer

  • You can shop the design to multiple installers for competitive bids
  • The designer is your advocate — they work for you, not for the installer
  • Better for very large or complex projects where an independent landscape architect adds value
  • Risk: the installer may not understand the designer’s intent, or may substitute cheaper materials

For most Austin residential projects ($15,000 to $80,000), design-build is the better approach. The coordination advantage outweighs the competitive bidding advantage, and the risk of miscommunication between a separate designer and installer costs more than any savings from competitive bids.

Step 3: Find Candidates

Start with three candidates. More than three creates analysis paralysis without adding useful information. Here is where to look:

  • Drive your neighborhood: If you see a landscape you admire, knock on the door and ask who built it. This is the most reliable method — you can see the finished product and ask the homeowner about their experience
  • Google reviews: Search “landscaper Austin TX” and read reviews. Look at the contractor’s response to negative reviews — it reveals more about their character than the positive ones
  • Nextdoor: Ask for recommendations. Pay attention to which names come up repeatedly
  • Instagram: Austin landscapers post their work on Instagram. Look for companies that show in-progress construction photos — not just glamour shots — because base preparation and construction quality are what matter most
  • Local nurseries: Staff at the Natural Gardener, Barton Springs Nursery, and Leaf Landscape Supply know who does good work because they see who buys quality materials

Avoid lead-generation sites that sell your contact information to a dozen contractors. You will spend weeks fielding calls from companies you never vetted.

Step 4: The Site Visit

A serious contractor will visit your property before providing an estimate. The site visit tells you more about the contractor than anything they say on the phone.

What a good contractor does during the site visit

  • Walks the entire property — not just the area you want to renovate. They are looking at drainage patterns, access, utilities, and how the project area relates to the rest of the property
  • Asks about drainage: Where does water go when it rains? Do you have standing water? Has the foundation had issues? A contractor who ignores drainage is going to build you a beautiful project that fails in two years
  • Looks at the soil: They should know whether you have Blackland clay, limestone, or something else. This affects base preparation depth, plant selection, and drainage design
  • Takes measurements: Rough pacing is acceptable for a ballpark. Serious contractors bring a tape measure or laser for actual dimensions
  • Asks about your budget: A contractor who refuses to discuss budget will design a $100,000 project when you have $30,000. Budget transparency is a sign of professionalism, not a negotiation tactic
  • Discusses timeline: When do you want it done? Are there events or deadlines? Can they start when you need them to?
  • Spends 30 to 60 minutes: A 10-minute walk-through is a red flag — they are not gathering enough information to provide an accurate estimate

What you should ask during the site visit

  1. How long have you been in business?
  2. Do you carry general liability and workers’ compensation insurance? Can I see a certificate?
  3. Who does the actual work — your own crews or subcontractors?
  4. Can I see 3 to 5 completed projects similar to mine?
  5. Can I talk to 2 to 3 recent clients?
  6. Do you handle design in-house or use an outside designer?
  7. Who will be my point of contact during the project?
  8. How do you handle changes or additions once the project starts?
  9. What is your warranty on plants? On hardscape?
  10. What is your typical lead time from contract to start?
  11. Do you pull permits when required?
  12. How do you handle Austin’s clay soil in base preparation?

Question 12 is the litmus test for Austin contractors. If they cannot explain their approach to clay soil — deeper gravel base, geotextile fabric, drainage provisions — they have not built enough projects on Austin clay to know what they are doing.

Step 5: Evaluate the Estimate

A professional estimate is a document, not a text message with a number. Here is what it should include:

Must-haves

  • Detailed scope of work: A written description of everything that will be done, in the order it will be done
  • Itemized materials: Material names, quantities, and specifications. “Pavers” is not specific enough — you need the manufacturer, product name, color, and square footage
  • Plant schedule: Species, container sizes, and quantities. “Assorted shrubs” is not a plant schedule
  • Site plan or design drawing: For projects over $5,000, the contractor should provide a scaled drawing showing the layout
  • Timeline: Estimated start date and completion date
  • Payment schedule: Tied to milestones, not front-loaded. A reasonable structure: 10–20% deposit, 30% when materials arrive, 30% at project midpoint, balance on completion
  • Warranty terms: Written warranty for plants (minimum 1 year) and hardscape (minimum 2 years)
  • Change order process: How additions or changes will be priced and approved

Comparing estimates

When comparing three estimates, the lowest bid is not always the best value. Here is what typically accounts for price differences:

Gravel base depth (Austin clay)4–6 inches8–12 inches
Geotextile fabricSkippedIncluded
Plant container size1 gallon3–5 gallon
Steel edgingPlastic or noneMild steel, 4–6 inch

The difference between a $20,000 estimate and a $30,000 estimate for the same project is almost always in the base preparation, material quality, plant sizes, and warranty — the things you cannot see once the project is finished but that determine whether it lasts 5 years or 25 years.

10 Red Flags That Should Eliminate a Contractor

  1. No insurance: If they cannot produce a current Certificate of Insurance, walk away. If an uninsured worker is injured on your property, you are liable
  2. Demands large upfront payment: More than 30% upfront is excessive. 50% or more is a red flag for financial instability — they may need your deposit to finish the last client’s job
  3. “We do not need a contract”: Everything should be in writing. Verbal agreements protect no one
  4. Cannot provide references: A contractor with 5+ years of experience should be able to give you 3 recent client references without hesitation
  5. Pressure tactics: “This price is only good today” or “We have one slot left this month” are sales tactics, not scheduling realities
  6. No site visit: A contractor who provides a quote over the phone or from Google Earth photos without walking the property is guessing, not estimating
  7. Vague about drainage: In Austin, drainage is the most important part of any hardscape project. A contractor who does not bring it up is either ignorant or planning to skip it
  8. “You do not need a permit” (before seeing the property): Permit requirements depend on the specific project and property. A blanket dismissal of permits is a red flag
  9. No portfolio: They should be able to show you completed projects — on their website, Instagram, or a physical portfolio. No photos means either no experience or results they are not proud of
  10. Subcontracts everything: If a contractor subcontracts 100% of the labor, they are a middleman, not a builder. You are paying a markup for someone who manages subcontractors, and quality control becomes their problem to manage

What to Expect During the Project

Once you hire a contractor, here is what a professional landscape installation looks like:

  • Pre-construction meeting: Walk the project with the contractor and crew lead. Confirm the scope, review the plan, mark utilities, and identify access points
  • Demo and site prep: Days 1–3 typically involve removing existing materials, grading, and excavation. This is the messiest phase
  • Base work: Gravel delivery, compaction, drainage installation. This is the most important phase — it should not be rushed
  • Hardscape installation: Patios, walls, edging, pathways. This is where the project takes shape
  • Irrigation: Drip zones and sprinkler heads installed and tested
  • Planting and mulch: Final phase. Plants go in according to the plan, followed by mulch
  • Final walkthrough: Walk the completed project with the contractor. Note any items that need adjustment. Do not make the final payment until you are satisfied

A good contractor communicates daily — either in person if the crew is on site, or via text or email with photos of progress. If you have to chase your contractor for updates, that is a problem.

Austin-Specific Considerations

  • Clay soil experience is non-negotiable: A contractor from Houston, Dallas, or out of state may not understand how Austin’s expansive Blackland clay affects base prep, drainage, and plant selection. Ask specifically about their experience with Austin clay soil
  • Water restrictions knowledge: Your contractor should design irrigation systems that comply with Austin Water’s one-day-per-week automatic sprinkler restriction. If they design a system that requires watering 3 days a week to keep plants alive, the plants will die when you follow the rules
  • Native plant knowledge: Austin is moving toward native and adapted plant palettes. A contractor who only knows how to install Asian jasmine and Indian hawthorn is working from a 1990s playbook
  • HOA compliance: If you are in an HOA, confirm the contractor is willing to submit plans for architectural review and work within HOA guidelines. Some contractors refuse HOA projects because of the approval process — find out before you sign a contract
  • Heat season scheduling: Projects installed in June through August face extreme heat stress on plants and workers. Fall (October–November) is the ideal installation season in Austin. Plan accordingly
