How to Keep Your Yard Alive in an Austin, TX Summer

July 3, 2026

Austin averages 45 days above 100°F per year. Add one-day-per-week watering restrictions, clay soil that cracks when it dries, and afternoon sun that hits like a broiler — and summer becomes the season that separates landscapes that are designed for Austin from landscapes that are fighting it. Here is how to keep your yard alive from June through September.

Summer Mowing Heights

Raise your mower. Taller grass shades the soil, reduces evaporation, and keeps roots 10–15°F cooler than scalped turf.

Grass TypeSummer Mowing HeightMow FrequencyNotes
St. Augustine3.5–4 inchesEvery 7–10 daysHighest setting on most mowers
Bermuda1.5–2 inchesEvery 5–7 daysHigher end during extreme heat
Zoysia2.5–3 inchesEvery 7–10 daysSlow growth reduces mowing
Buffalo grass3–4 inchesEvery 10–14 daysCan go unmowed for weeks

The one-third rule: Never remove more than one-third of the blade length in a single mowing. If your St. Augustine is 6 inches tall and your target is 4 inches, take it to 4.5 first, then mow again in 3–4 days. Scalping exposes soil to direct sun and can kill turf that was otherwise surviving.

Watering Strategy

Under Conservation Stage restrictions, automatic irrigation gets one day per week. Make it count.

How to water on your one day

  1. Run each zone twice: Once in the early morning (5–6 a.m.) and once in the evening (7–8 p.m.). This delivers approximately 1 inch total
  2. Cycle and soak: Run each zone for 10–12 minutes, wait 30–60 minutes for absorption, then run again. This prevents runoff on clay soil
  3. Target 6–8 inches of penetration: Push a screwdriver into the soil after watering. It should slide in easily to 6 inches. If it stops at 2 inches, your run time is too short
  4. Check soil before watering: If the soil is still moist 3 inches down, skip the cycle and let the zone rest

Hand watering

Hand watering with a handheld hose is allowed any day, any time under all restriction stages. Use this for:

  • Spot-watering stressed areas that the irrigation system misses
  • New plantings that need daily moisture to establish
  • Container plants that dry out daily in summer
  • Rescue watering for trees showing drought stress (leaf curl, early leaf drop)

Mulch: Your Most Important Tool

Three to four inches of hardwood mulch in all landscape beds is the single most effective thing you can do for your plants in summer. Mulch:

  • Reduces soil temperature by 10–20°F compared to bare soil
  • Cuts evaporation by 50–70%, meaning your plants need far less water
  • Prevents weed germination so plants do not compete for moisture
  • Feeds the soil as it decomposes, improving clay soil structure over time

Check mulch depth in May. If it has decomposed below 2 inches, top it up to 3–4 inches before summer hits. Keep mulch 3–4 inches away from tree trunks and plant stems to prevent rot.

What You CAN Do in Summer

Summer is not a dead zone for landscaping. It is the best time for projects that do not need water:

  • Build hardscape: Patios, stone work, retaining walls, and steel edging
  • Install landscape lighting: Long evenings make aiming fixtures easier
  • Build pergolas and outdoor kitchens: Steel fabrication and installation work year-round
  • Plan and design: Use summer to finalize designs so you are ready to plant in the fall window
  • Repair irrigation: Fix broken heads, adjust coverage, and replace worn nozzles before fall planting
  • Build fences: Fencing does not require water and installs in any weather

Irrigation System Maintenance

A malfunctioning irrigation system wastes water, violates restrictions, and leaves parts of your lawn without coverage. Summer maintenance checklist:

  1. Run each zone manually and walk the yard. Look for broken heads, misaligned nozzles, and dry spots
  2. Check for runoff: If water is flowing onto the sidewalk or street, reduce run time and add a cycle-and-soak delay
  3. Replace worn nozzles: Spray heads lose coverage as nozzles wear. Replace any head that sprays a mist instead of distinct streams
  4. Inspect drip zones: Clear clogged emitters, check for line damage from landscaping or animals
  5. Verify controller schedule: Make sure your controller runs on the correct day and during allowed hours
  6. Install a rain sensor: Prevents the system from running during and after rainfall

The Long-Term Solution

If you spend every summer fighting to keep your yard alive, the problem is not your maintenance routine — it is your landscape design. A yard that depends on three watering days per week to survive is not designed for Austin. The long-term solution is a landscape that looks good on one watering day or less: xeriscape beds, native plants, gravel zones, reduced turf areas, and hardscape that never needs water.
